# O3N: Omnidirectional Open-Vocabulary Occupancy Prediction 

Mengfei Duan 1,∗, Hao Shi 2,∗, Fei Teng 1, Guoqiang Zhao 1, Yuheng Zhang 1,Zhiyong Li 1,†, and Kailun Yang 1,†

Abstract. Understanding and reconstructing the 3D world through om-nidirectional perception is an inevitable trend in the development of au-tonomous agents and embodied intelligence. However, existing 3D oc-cupancy prediction methods are constrained by limited perspective in-puts and predefined training distribution, making them difficult to apply to embodied agents that require comprehensive and safe perception of scenes in open world exploration. To address this, we present O3N, the first purely visual, end-to-end Omnidirectional Open-vocabulary Occup-ancy predictio N framework. O3N embeds omnidirectional voxels in a polar-spiral topology via the Polar-spiral Mamba (PsM) module, en-abling continuous spatial representation and long-range context mod-eling across 360 °. The Occupancy Cost Aggregation (OCA) module in-troduces a principled mechanism for unifying geometric and semantic supervision within the voxel space, ensuring consistency between the reconstructed geometry and the underlying semantic structure. More-over, Natural Modality Alignment (NMA) establishes a gradient-free alignment pathway that harmonizes visual features, voxel embeddings, and text semantics, forming a consistent “pixel-voxel-text” representation triad. Extensive experiments on multiple models demonstrate that our method not only achieves state-of-the-art performance on QuadOcc and Human360Occ benchmarks but also exhibits remarkable cross-scene gen-eralization and semantic scalability, paving the way toward universal 3D world modeling. The source code will be made publicly available at O3N. 

Keywords: Panoramic Image · Open-vocabulary Occupancy · Scene Understanding

Thus, open-vocabulary understanding is crucial, as it enables the identification and prediction of unknown object categories without prior knowledge [7, 23, 63], facilitating flexible and scalable semantic representation. 2 Related Work 

Panoramic semantic scene understanding. Panoramic understanding aims for comprehensive scene perception but is challenged by distortions and un-4 M. Duan, H. Shi et al. 

even sampling [29] in omnidirectional images. For panoramic semantic segmen-tation, early methods project panoramic images into several patches for in-dependent segmentation [66, 67]. Subsequent studies have explored Unsuper-vised Domain Adaptation (UDA) methods, including explicit [31, 86, 88] and implicit [74, 75, 85, 87] knowledge alignment as well as pseudo-label genera-tion [77, 78, 89], to enable more reliable panoramic segmentation under lim-ited supervision. In addition, several specialized architectures are proposed for panoramic segmentation [5, 13, 14, 19, 21, 22, 26, 37, 47, 64, 72]. For panoramic se-mantic mapping, it aims to transform panoramic inputs into Bird’s-Eye-View (BEV) representations, where the inputs can be either surround-view fisheye images [24, 30, 42, 70] or a single panoramic image [10, 50, 58, 76]. Recently, re-search has shifted towards 3D semantic occupancy prediction, with multi-sensor, multi-view approaches achieving significant progress. By combining multiple fish-eye cameras [8, 38], depth information [60], and point clouds [68, 84, 94], these approaches greatly enhance semantic understanding and spatial geometry mod-eling in dynamic environments. Further, OneOcc [43] implements purely visual panoramic semantic occupancy prediction for legged robots. Despite significant progress in panoramic 3D semantic modeling trained on predefined categories, existing methods lack the ability to recognize unseen semantics, leading to lim-itations when encountering unknown scenes in the open world. 

Open-vocabulary occupancy prediction. A dominant line of work in 3D occupancy prediction follows a closed-set paradigm, where models are trained to recognize predefined semantic categories from LiDAR or multi-view inputs [4, 15, 27, 44, 52, 59, 92]. These approaches focus on efficient spatial representa-tion [16,17,32,49,79], contextual reasoning and semantic generalization [9,36,73], yet remain limited to fixed vocabularies. Open-vocabulary occupancy prediction methods enable models to predict the occupancy of new, unseen object cate-gories from natural language input without explicit labels. OVO [48] pioneered a framework for open-vocabulary occupancy by extracting knowledge from a frozen 2D open-vocabulary segmenter and CLIP [40] text encoder into a 3D model. Subsequent research primarily focuses on multimodal fusion [18], self-supervised learning [11,53], foundation model adaptation [83,91], neural radiance fields ensembles [2], progressive gaussian modeling [65, 90], and pseudo-labeled reconstruction [25, 71] to reinforce open-vocabulary perception. Unlike existing methods that rely on fixed input perspectives and predefined training categories, our method leverages flexible omnidirectional vision and open-vocabulary under-standing to break such rigid limitations, enabling more robust generalizable scene comprehension beyond traditional closed-world assumptions.

To more comprehensively advance the understanding of open world, unlike other multi-modal and -stage methods [11, 18, 53, 71, 83], we introduce the first purely visual, end-to-end omnidirectional open-vocabulary occupancy prediction framework, providing a new solution for comprehensive scene understanding. Specifically, O3N includes the following components: (1) a visual feature ex-tractor for omnidirectional images; (2) a 2D-to-3D view transformation that gen-erates both 3D cube and cylindrical voxels; (3) a 3D decoder capable of learning fine-grained spatial geometry and semantic information; and (4) an occupancy prediction head. Through the close integration of these modules, O3N can not only perform closed-set semantic occupancy prediction but also demonstrate strong transferability. Furthermore, the proposed Polar-spiral Mamba (PsM, see Sec. 3.1) module enhances omnidirectional spatial perception capabilities effec-tively. For 3D open-vocabulary perception, the Occupancy Cost Aggregation (OCA, Sec. 3.2) and a gradient-free Natural Modality Alignment (NMA, Sec. 3.3) technique are proposed to enforce the “pixel-voxel-text” semantic consistency, and effectively mitigate overfitting. This unified design not only enhances perception completeness but also endows 3D occupancy prediction with semantic plasticity: a key step toward scalable world modeling. 

3.1 Polar-spiral Mamba Module 

Due to the limitations of angular partitioning, cylindrical voxels exhibit inherent discontinuity in data format, leading to a disruption in spatial continuity near the poles, which contradicts the original intent ( i.e ., spatial perception originates from the center, with higher precision in closer regions). Standard 3D convolution struggles to adapt to this critical characteristic, and the high computational cost 6 M. Duan, H. Shi et al. 

of transformers is also prohibitive for real-world deployment. To preserve the continuity of angular space effectively, as shown in Fig. 4, we propose the Polar-spiral Mamba (PsM) module with a dual-branch framework to better utilize the intrinsic spatial structure of omnidirectional images. We utilizes Spatial-Mamba [62] to capture the intricate structures within the polar coordinate system, which offers transformer-like long sequence modeling capabilities with linear complexity. Specifically, the cylindrical voxels represen-tation Vp ∈ RC×R×P ×Z is compressed into Bird’s-Eye View (BEV) feature 

Bp ∈ RC×R×P , reducing spatial redundancy and improving feature representa-tion efficiency. Then, the proposed P-SMamba scans the polar space in a spiral pattern (see the red dashed line in Fig. 4), starting from the pole and progres-sively increasing the radius. The scanning path aligns well with the properties of omnidirectional imaging, especially in the south pole regions, where informa-tion density gradually changes. 3.2 Occupancy Cost Aggregation 

The uneven data distribution and sampling imbalance intensifies the risk of over-fitting in simple feature alignment strategies, resulting in misalignment of the joint embedding space for unseen classes. To address this, we propose construct-ing a refined occupancy cost to characterize the relationship between voxel and text embeddings, thereby avoiding direct discrete feature alignment. O3N 7

In particular, analogous to the image–text matching cost [7], the occupancy cost is defined as the similarity between 3D voxel embeddings V and text embed-dings T, typically measured via cosine similarity. Intuitively, the occupancy cost volume can be viewed as rough 3D semantic masks grounded to their respective classes, which is calculated as follows: 

C(i, l ) = Vi · Tl

Then, we feed the occupancy cost to a single 3D convolution layer, which processes each cost slice C(: , l ) ∈ RH×W ×D×1 independently, to obtain initial occupancy cost embedding Oc ∈ RH×W ×D×Nc×do refer to [7]. Nc

represents the number of cost slices, and do is the embedding dimension. Furthermore, to enhance spatial smoothness within the voxels and model the relationship of the local region, we employ Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling (ASPP) [6], which is beneficial to fuse information from different receptive fields, and apply spatial aggregation for each class individually. Subsequent to spatial aggregation, a linear transformer [20] for class aggregation is applied to consider the text modality, explicitly capturing relationships between different classes. Similarly, we further leverage the voxel feature V3D and text embeddings T to provide spatial structure or contextual information of the input, thereby guid-ing the overall process. Ultimately, the output is fed into the prediction head, with a residual connection for final inference. More architectural details of the lightweight occupancy cost aggregator can be seen in the supplement. However, we observe that directly supervising the aggregation learning with cross-entropy loss leads to isolated voxel semantic mappings, thereby weakening the generalization ability of the model. To tackle this issue, we utilize the scene affinity loss [4] to capture the semantic correlations among voxels. 3.3 Natural Modality Alignment 

Although CLIP exhibits strong generalization ability, it struggles with the in-herent domain gap between image and text embeddings, even after extensive 8 M. Duan, H. Shi et al. 

alignment during training [48, 82]. This issue is further exacerbated by the pro-jection errors inherent to panoramic images. Under the pixel-voxel feature align-ment, the “pixel-voxel-text” semantic consistency cannot be effectively guaran-teed, thereby struggling to recognize novel semantics. Moreover, since only base class semantics are available during training, directly employing a learning-based strategy will inevitably develop excessive reliance on the distribution of seen se-mantics while deteriorating the ability to understand unlimited semantics. To overcome these issues, we propose a gradient-free natural modality gap alignment strategy between text embeddings and semantic prototypes prior to occupancy cost aggregation. Specifically, it iteratively aggregates text embed-dings with prototypes of seen semantics, which are obtained by filtering the base class pixel embeddings with the Exponential Moving Average (EMA) method [3] during the training phase, achieving co-optimization in shared embedding space until convergence. 4.1 Experimental Setups Datasets. We evaluate methods on two omnidirectional occupancy datasets: QuadOcc [43] and Human360Occ [43]. QuadOcc is a real-world first-person 360 ◦

occupancy dataset on a quadruped robot within a campus environment. It has 

6 semantic classes with an additional empty class on a 64 ×64 ×8 grid ( 0.4m). Human360Occ (H3O) is a CARLA-based human-ego 360 ◦ occupancy dataset with simulated gait. It uses 10 classes along with an empty class for training, 10 M. Duan, H. Shi et al. 

supports with-city (Homo, per-map 8:2 train/val) and cross-city (Heter, default 

12 /4 maps) splits. More details about the datasets, class distributions, and ex-amples are provided in the supplement. 

Class Split. Following mainstream open-vocabulary occupancy setups [25, 48], we choose vehicle , road , and building as novel classes in QuadOcc, which is nearly 68% of the total voxels. The remaining classes, e.g ., person , vegetation ,and terrain , are considered base classes. In Human360Occ, the road , sidewalk ,

building , car , truck , bus , and two wheeler are chosen as novel classes, which is ap-proximately 75% of the total voxels. The remaining classes are the base classes, specifically vegetation , person , and pole . In particular, a significant proportion of these base classes are extremely rare in the datasets, with some classes repre-senting less than 1% of the total voxels. This presents a considerable challenge during both training and inference. However, its low labeling cost also provides the possibility for subsequent data expansion and task expansion.

Implementation Details. Our proposed method is transferable to any occu-pancy network. Effectiveness of NMA. Building upon the omnidirectional geometry-semantic consistency cost volume, NMA introduces a gradient-free alignment mechanism that avoids interference caused by modality gaps or noise in vision-language model predictions. Crucially, it decouples text-branch training, enabling the con-struction of a consistent and generalizable “P-V-T” representational triplet. To further elucidate its impact, Fig. 6 shows the distribution of similarity scores between voxels and text embeddings with and without NMA during inference. O3N demonstrates a notably tighter and more stable distribution, with the ma-jority of samples concentrated within the narrow range of 0.93 ∼0.97 . In con-trast, the w/o NMA variant shows a significantly broader distribution, with a non-negligible fraction of low-similarity samples, suggesting that its cross-modal alignment is more susceptible to noise and exhibits weaker generalization. This observation further corroborates the effectiveness of NMA in enhancing semantic generalization capability in omnidirectional open-vocabulary occupancy.

“pixel–voxel–text” representations, thereby markedly enhancing cross-modal se-mantic consistency and generalization to unseen novel categories. 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce the omnidirectional open-vocabulary occupancy pre-diction task for the first time and propose O3N, the first purely visual, end-to-end framework to unify omnidirectional visual perception, spatial geometry, and open-vocabulary semantic prediction. It enables seamless continuous spa-tial perception and long-range context modeling across 360 ° via the proposed Polar-spiral Mamba (PsM) module. Furthermore, the Occupancy Cost predic-tion (OCA) offers a unified geometric and semantic learning mechanism, en-suring that the reconstructed geometry closely aligns with the underlying se-mantics. The Natural Modality Alignment (NMA) synchronizes visual features, voxel embeddings, and textual semantics, resulting in a consistent, robust “pixel-voxel-text” triplet representation. O3N achieves state-of-the-art open-vocabulary occupancy prediction under omnidirectional visual perception, offering a new perspective for comprehensive scene understanding in open-world scenarios.

A.2 Cubic vs. Cylindrical Voxel Projections 

We visualize how different voxel parameterizations project onto an equirectan-gular omnidirectional image in QuadOcc. In Fig. S2, the points indicate the pro-jected voxel centers, and the color bars show the normalized depth distribution of 22 M. Duan, H. Shi et al. 

occupied voxels along each azimuth ray. Traditional axis-aligned Cartesian cubic voxels, when projected onto a panoramic image in the world coordinate system, form fan-shaped regions: voxels at different heights yield similar depth patterns along inclined rays, and distant structures, such as roads, terrain, and building facades, are compressed into narrow bands near the equatorial zone. While this configuration enables uniform metric sampling in 3D space, it disrupts the angu-lar regularity inherent to panoramic imagery. In contrast, camera-centric omni-directional cylindrical voxels employ equal step sizes in both azimuth and height, producing nearly horizontal sampling rows whose projections follow the equidis-tant cylindrical-projection parameterization and maintain the circular continuity of distant structures. This complementarity motivates combining the two voxel meshes and applying the PsM module to jointly preserve near-field metric fidelity and far-field azimuthal coherence. near far near far near far

B.1 Datasets 

We evaluate methods on two omnidirectional occupancy datasets: QuadOcc [43] and Human360Occ [43]. Fig. S4 illustrates sample omnidirectional images from the QuadOcc and Human360Occ datasets, along with the corresponding voxel frequency distributions across semantic classes, providing a clear overview of their scene diversity and inherent class imbalance. QuadOcc is a real-world first-person 360 ◦ occupancy dataset on a quadruped robot within a campus envi-ronment. It contains 7 scenes for training, 3 scenes for validation/test, totaling 

24 K frames standardized day/dusk/night coverage. It has 6 semantic classes with an additional empty class on a 64 ×64 ×8 grid ( 0.4m). Each sample covers a range of [−12 .8m, −12 .8m, −1.2m, 12 .8m, 12 .8m, 2.0m], with a fine voxel size of 

[0 .4m, 0.4m, 0.4m] on a 64 ×64 ×8 grid, reflecting embodied compute and lower speed than cars. Human360Occ (H3O) is a CARLA-based human-ego 360 ◦ oc-cupancy dataset with simulated gait. H3O contains 160 sequences, totaling 8K

frames over 16 maps and multiple weathers/times. Each sample covers a range of [−12 .8m, −12 .8m, −2.4m, 12 .8m, 12 .8m, 0.8m], provides semantic occupancy labels at 64 ×64 ×8 (native) and 128 ×128 ×16 grid. It uses 10 classes along with an empty class for training, supports with-city (Homo, per-map 8:2 train/val) and cross-city (Heter, default 12 /4 maps) splits.

D Discussions 

D.1 Limitations and Future Work 

The performance of omnidirectional open-vocabulary occupancy prediction is de-pendent on the vision-language models, but a vision-language model tailored for omnidirectional vision is scarce in the literature. The extracted open-vocabulary 2D image pixel features are prone to semantic shifts, limited generalization, and distribution bias in valid voxel sampling. Future research could explore self-supervised learning methods for omnidirectional visual scenes to enhance the generalizability in semantic understanding. Despite these limitations, O3N ad-vances comprehensive scene understanding by unifying omnidirectional visual perception with 3D geometric-semantic prediction under open-ended semantics within a purely visual, end-to-end framework. D.2 Failure Case Analysis 

Fig. S7 presents two typical failure cases of O3N. Depth ambiguity is an inherent challenge in monocular 3D reconstruction. While depth-based Lift-Splat-Shoot (LSS) projection [39] is common in pinhole vision, panoramic depth estimation contains substantial noise in the predicted depth maps, which severely compro-mises spatial perception accuracy. Given these challenges, we adopt the Fea-tures Line of Sight 2D-to-3D Projection (FLoSP) projection strategy [4, 33], verified in OneOcc [43], as it is more concise and robust than learnable LSS [39] and cross-attention mechanism [28], and optimal for panoramic settings. How-ever, the FLoSP method is insensitive to overall spatial depth, so in the first case, nearly all methods using FLoSP exhibit misalignment in spatial range per-ception. Specifically, when the sidewalk is adjacent to vegetation, all methods demonstrate additional prediction errors. Furthermore, due to the visual similar-ity between the pole and sidewalk, both MonoScene and OVO fail to detect the pole, while O3N accurately reconstructs the occupancy state of the pole. This 28 M. Duan, H. Shi et al. 

demonstrates that O3N can effectively perceive both geometric and semantic information in space, thereby showcasing enhanced generalization ability. Omnidirectional Image Ground Truth MonoScene OVO Ours (O3N) Omnidirectional Image Ground Truth MonoScene OVO Ours (O3N) 

Fig. S7: Failure case analysis on H3O Homo (with-city). Similar to MonoScene [48], O3N employs the same 2D-to-3D projection strategy (FLoSP), re-sulting in limited sensitivity to spatial depth. This limitation causes inaccuracies in accurately perceiving the full spatial extent of the scene. 

In the second case, in a rainy street scene at dusk, due to the dim lighting and interference from rain-reflected light, MonoScene misidentifies most of the sidewalk as unoccupied ( empty ). OVO and O3N also encounter similar issues, but, compared to OVO, O3N more robustly reconstructs the occupancy state of the entire scene and demonstrates clearer semantic awareness. For example, OVO reconstructs the sidewalk as a building, while O3N reconstructs it as road, which is more semantically similar to the sidewalk. These failures demonstrate that while current 2D-to-3D projection strate-gies can effectively extend omnidirectional images into 3D space, they still have inherent limitations in spatial localization and are prone to structural misjudg-ments. Therefore, enhancing the spatial depth perception of models to miti-gate projection-induced geometric biases constitutes an important direction for future research. Furthermore, to address complex environmental factors, such as insufficient lighting and reflections caused by rain or snow, large-scale data augmentation and domain adaptation across different weather conditions and scenes can improve the robustness and generalization performance, especially in extreme weather and previously unseen scenarios. 

D.3 Societal Impacts 

Omnidirectional open-vocabulary occupancy prediction has the potential to sig-nificantly enhance the safety of autonomous driving systems and the perceptual intelligence of smart devices, while also accelerating the adoption of embodied AI technologies in healthcare, manufacturing, and service robotics. However, the method remains imperfect in prediction accuracy, and in practical applications, these limitations and the associated uncertainties must be carefully considered.
