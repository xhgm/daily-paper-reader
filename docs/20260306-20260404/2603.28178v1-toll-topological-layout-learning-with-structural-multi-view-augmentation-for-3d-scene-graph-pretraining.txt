# ToLL: Topological Layout Learning with Structural Multi-view Augmentation for 3D Scene Graph Pretraining 

Yucheng Huang * 1 Luping Ji 1 Xiangwei Jiang * 1 Wen Li 1 Mao Ye 1

Abstract 

3D Scene Graph (3DSG) generation plays a piv-otal role in spatial understanding and semantic-affordance perception. However, its generalizabil-ity is often constrained by data scarcity. Current solutions primarily focus on cross-modal assisted representation learning and object-centric gener-ation pre-training. The former relies heavily on predicate annotations, while the latter’s predicate learning may be bypassed due to strong object priors. Consequently, they could not often pro-vide a label-free and robust self-supervised proxy task for 3DSG fine-tuning. To bridge this gap, we propose a Topological Layout Learning (ToLL) for 3DSG pretraining framework. In detail, we de-sign an Anchor-Conditioned Topological Geome-try Reasoning, with a GNN to recover the global layout of zero-centered subgraphs by the spatial priors from sparse anchors. This process is strictly modulated by predicate features, thereby enforc-ing the predicate relation learning. Furthermore, we construct a Structural Multi-view Augmenta-tion to avoid semantic corruption, and enhancing representations via self-distillation. The extensive experiments on 3DSSG dataset demonstrate that our ToLL could improve representation quality, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines.

By capturing the semantic associations and func-tional affordances between entities, 3DSGs serve as a criti-cal structured representation for downstream agents in tasks like Vision-Language Navigation (Anderson et al., 2018) and Robotics (Zitkovich et al., 2023). Current 3DSG research primarily explores knowledge prior injection, predicate representation modeling, and self-supervised pretext tasks. Early approaches relied on knowledge prior injection, either through statistical prototypes (Zhang et al., 2021b) or cross-modal distillation from CLIP (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Koch et al., 2024a) as shown in Figure 1(a). However, these methods depend heavily on annotated text-label pairs, limiting scalability. To reduce annotation dependence, Self-Supervised Learn-ing (SSL) has emerged as a promising direction. Van Den Oord et al., 2017), (Koch et al., 2024b) designs an augmentation-free generative pretraining for 3DSG as shown in Figure 1(b). However, we identify a critical flaw in existing generative graph approaches: the ”Geometric Short-cut”. When dense spatial priors are provided, models tend to trivially interpolate the node positions from neighbors or themselves, rather than decoding the underlying topological constraints, leading to superficial edge representations. To address these limitations, we propose a Topological Lay-out Learning (ToLL) framework as shown in Figure 1(c). Our approach enforces the model to learn geometry strictly from topology, preventing shortcut learning. First, we intro-duce Anchor-Conditioned Topological Geometric Reason-ing (ACTGR). By normalizing node coordinates and enforc-ing a single-anchor information bottleneck (Hu et al., 2024), we compel the GNN to function as a ”dead-reckoning” system. The model must recover the global layout solely through edge-based propagation, establishing a generative feedback loop that simultaneously enhances fine-grained geometry and edge semantics. Second, to robustify representation against the semantic cor-ruption from geometric transformation, we propose Struc-tural Multi-view Augmentation (SMA) inspired by (Caron et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2025b; You et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021; Hou et al., 2022; Zhou et al.). Instead of geometric distortion, we employ connectivity perturbation to generate asymmetric student views. Through a SwAV-style (Caron et al., 2020) self-distillation, we enforce semantic consis-tency, ensuring the backbone captures representations invari-ant to both spatial incompleteness and topological occlusion. In summary, our primary contributions are: (1) We propose the ToLL, a new pre-training framework that mitigates geometric shortcuts in 3DSG generation via an information bottleneck. (2) We design ACTGR to enforce the explicit topological reasoning for layout recovery, and SMA to learn structurally robust semantics via cross-view self-distillation. (3) The extensive experiments conducted on the public 3DSSG dataset demonstrate that our ToLL scheme effec-tively boosts 3DSG fine-tuning. 

2. Related Work 

3D Scene Graph Prediction. Supervised 3DSG architec-tures (Wald et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023) have evolved to incorporate external semantic priors (e.g., VLMs (Koch et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024)) or explicit geometric constraints (Wu et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2025) for predicate refinement. However, these methods rely heavily on sparse annotated triplets, limiting generalization. 

Self-supervised Learning on Point Cloud. Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) on point clouds, including joint-embedding (Xie et al., 2020; Zhang et al.) and masked modeling (Pang et al., 2023), addresses data scarcity but struggles in the 3DSG domain. Rigid geometric augmenta-tions (e.g., rotation) used in SSL can fundamentally alter spa-tial predicate semantics. Furthermore, these object-centric methods (Wu et al., 2025a) fail to capture the complex topological structures essential for inferring inter-object re-lationships. Despite (Koch et al., 2024b)’s success with generative pre-training, it ignores shortcuts arising from full node visibility, which undermine topological reasoning. This limitation prevents the model from acquiring effective predicate representation capabilities. 

Self-supervised Learning (SSL) on Graph. Graph SSL (Liu et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021) typically constructs multi-view pairs via topological augmentations—such as node dropping or edge perturbation employed in GraphCL (You et al., 2020; 2021; Xu et al., 2021), or through reconstructive objectives like masking node features and structures in GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023). Since 3DSG can be abstracted as a graph representa-tion learning problem (Hamilton, 2020), these topological augmentations offer a crucial advantage. By constructing multiple structural views, we can facilitate robust represen-tation learning while avoiding the semantic corruption of predicates often caused by the geometric augmentations.

4. Methods 

We formulate 3D Scene Graph pre-training (Figure 2) as a diffusion-based conditional layout restoration task. To achieve the context-aware learning of semantics and geome-try, we propose Anchor-Conditioned Topological Geometric Reasoning (ACTGR). Furthermore, we design Structural Multi-view Augmentation (SMA) to facilitate robust repre-sentation learning in a joint-embedding way.

Topology-Forced Single-Anchor Training. In layout learning, relational networks face a critical issue, it tend to exploit shortcuts by explicit spatial priors from multiple an-chors, rather than learning the relative geometric constraints encoded in edges Esub . 4.2. Structural Multi-view Augmentation 

To facilitate robust representation learning, we introduce 

Structural Multi-view Augmentation (SMA) . Unlike stan-dard geometric jittering, SMA employs topological perturba-tion and spatial masking to construct complementary views, enforcing semantic consistency via self-distillation. We construct three structural views that interact via a student-teacher paradigm in the following: 

Edge-Guided Student View, Gedge : This view preserves the complete edge topology but almost lacks absolute spa-tial attributes for the nodes (masking nodes). It aligns with the reference to learn geometry-to-semantics mapping. 

Holistic Teacher View, Gref : A global view processed by the Target network (EMA). It retains both rich node informa-tion and stable edge topology, serving as the comprehensive upper bound for the students. 

Node-Guided Student View, Gnode : This view preserves the rich node information but suffers from severe topologi-cal occlusion (masking edges). It forces the encoder to rely on node features to infer missing relationships. It aligns with the reference to learn semantics-to-topology reasoning. 

Asymmetric Cross-View Distillation. We adopt a Mean Teacher framework consisting of a student network fθ and a target network fξ . To prevent representational collapse, we employ an asymmetric architecture where a predictor head 

gϕ is appended exclusively to the student branch. The target network parameters ξ are updated via Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of the student parameters θ:

ξt = αξ t−1 + (1 − α)θt (9) where α is the momentum coefficient.

5. Experiments 

5.1. Pretraining Setup 

For pre-training, we constructed subgraph samples using 1,513 ScanNet scenes. We first excluded objects with fewer than 512 points. The remaining objects were uniformly sam-pled to 1,024 points. Furthermore, we construct connected subgraphs to generate 7,392 samples, which collectively contain 33,949 nodes and 61,599 edges. The pre-training stage consists of 150 epochs. The AdamW optimizer is utilized with a weight decay of 10 −4 and a learning rate of 10 −3, modulated by a cosine scheduler with a 5-epoch warm-up. A weight of 0.1 is assigned to the self-distillation loss component. The batchsize is set to 32. 5.2. Fine-tuning Setup 

3DSSG dataset is employed as the benchmark for scene graph fine-tuning. The optimizer and scheduler configura-tions remained consistent with the pre-training phase, except that the warm-up period was omitted and the batch size is set to 8. Scene Graph Classification (SGCLs, with graph-constrained) and Predicate Classifica-tion (PredCls) are introduced to evaluate predicate recall capability - both employing the Top-k recall “R@k”. In addition, we introduce two evaluation protocols: 

The 1st protocol is a full fine-tuning scheme. In this setting, the parameters of the complete pretrained encoders are updatable; however, we assigned them a lower learning rate of 0.5 × 10 −4, while the MLP layers are set to 10 −4.

The 2nd protocol is an MLP-only fine-tuning scheme. 

The entire pre-trained encoders are frozen, and only the MLP layers are fine-tuned with a learning rate, 10 −4.

6. Conclusion and Future Work 

This paper presents a new Topological Layout Learning with Structural Multi-view Augmentation framework for the pre-training of 3DSG generation. By integrating Anchor-Conditioned Topological Geometric Reasoning with the self-distillation on Structural Multi-view Augmentation, it turns a layout recovery task into a robust proxy for learning topological and semantic dependencies. Extensive experi-ments show that this method could achieve state-of-the-art performance on 3DSSG benchmark. Nevertheless, some challenges still exist, due to the inherent sparsity of data and the high noise levels in relational triplet data. C.3. Details on Noise-Aware Focal Loss for Layout Recovery 

In the layout recovery task, our ultimate recovery target is not the zero-centered object point clouds, but rather the subgraph point clouds endowed with absolute spatial positional information, objects in these subgraphs naturally exhibit significant variations in scale (e.g., large structural walls vs. small household objects) and data quality (e.g., sensor noise vs. clean surfaces). Standard L2 loss treats all points equally, which leads to two optimization issues: • Dominance of Outliers: Noisy points with high local variance produce large distance errors, dominating the gradient and causing the model to overfit to noise rather than recovering the underlying geometry. • Scale Imbalance: Without proper weighting, dense small objects might be overwhelmed by sparse large structures, or vice versa. To address this, we implement a Variance-Aware Focal Loss mechanism. Instead of globally normalizing the point clouds (which destroys relative layout information), we compute adaptive weights based on scale-invariant local geometric properties. This allows the network to focus on reliable structural components while suppressing the influence of high-variance noise. C.3.1. L OCAL GEOMETRIC FEATURE EXTRACTION

D.2. Analysis of Different Student View Numbers 

To investigate the impact of view diversity on representation learning, we conducted an ablation study by varying the number of augmented student views ( K) used in our Structural Multi-view Augmentation (SMA) module. In our default setting, we utilize K = 3 views to construct the student-teacher consistency constraints. As shown in the first block of Table 6, increasing the number of student views consistently improves the model’s performance across all metrics. Specifically, when increasing K from 3 to 4, and subsequently to 5, the Object A@1 score rises from 58.62% to 59.24%, and the Predicate mA@1 increases from 54.59% to 55.78%. This trend suggests that introducing more diverse student views forces the network to learn more robust and invariant geometric representations. With a higher K, the model is exposed to a wider variety of spatial perturbations and masking patterns during pre-training, which effectively prevents overfitting to specific local features and encourages the capture of global structural dependencies. Although K = 5 yields the best results, it also incurs higher computational costs; therefore, we adopt K = 3 as the default trade-off between efficiency and performance in our main experiments. 

D.3. Analysis of Noise-Aware Focal Loss 

A core challenge in 3D scene layout recovery is the presence of high-variance noise and the scale imbalance between different scene components. To address this, we proposed the Noise-Aware Focal Loss (NAFL), which dynamically down-weights noisy points and up-weights structurally significant regions based on local geometric properties. The second block of Table 6 compares our NAFL against a standard L2 reconstruction loss (MSE). The results demonstrate that replacing the standard loss with NAFL provides a significant performance boost. The Object A@1 improves by 1.42% (from 57.20% to 58.62%), and the Predicate mA@1 sees gain of 1.19% (from 53.40% to 54.59%). This improvement confirms our hypothesis that standard L2 loss is easily dominated by outliers with high local variance, leading to suboptimal convergence. By incorporating the variance-aware weighting mechanism, NAFL effectively suppresses the gradients from noisy unstructured points while focusing the model’s optimization on valid geometric surfaces (e.g., walls, furniture), thereby resulting in a more accurate and semantically meaningful scene graph generation.
