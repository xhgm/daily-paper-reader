# Semi-Supervised Few-Shot Adaptation of Vision-Language Models 

Julio Silva-Rodríguez and Ender Konukoglu 

Computer Vision Lab, ETH Zurich, Zurich, Switzerland 

jusilva@ethz.ch 

Abstract. Vision-language models (VLMs) pre-trained on large, het-erogeneous data sources are becoming increasingly popular, providing rich multi-modal embeddings that enable efficient transfer to new tasks. A particularly relevant application is few-shot adaptation, where only a handful of annotated examples are available to adapt the model through multi-modal linear probes. In medical imaging, specialized VLMs have shown promising performance in zero- and few-shot image classification, which is valuable for mitigating the high cost of expert annotations. However, challenges remain in extremely low-shot regimes: the inherent class imbalances in medical tasks often lead to underrepresented cat-egories, penalizing overall model performance. To address this limita-tion, we propose leveraging unlabeled data by introducing an efficient semi-supervised solver that propagates text-informed pseudo-labels dur-ing few-shot adaptation. The proposed method enables lower-budget an-notation pipelines for adapting VLMs, reducing labeling effort by ≥ 50% 

in low-shot regimes. Code is available: SS-Text-U. 

Keywords: Medical VLMs · Few-shot · Semi-supervised learning. 

## 1 Introduction 

Foundation models, particularly contrastive vision-language models (VLMs), are being increasingly adopted in computer vision. These learn flexible multi-modal embedding representations that can be transferred to downstream tasks, and have shown improved robustness across domain shifts and data-efficient adap-tation capabilities [22]. The limited performance of generalist VLMs in visually fine-grained, specialized domains such as medical imaging has motivated the development of modality-specific medical VLMs, e.g., for histology [17], oph-thalmology [32], or radiology [43]. A widely recognized use case for these VLMs in medical image computing is few-shot classification, i.e., using only a small number of labeled images to adapt the model to a new dataset/task (so-called shots), given the high cost of gathering expert-level annotations. First developed in the computer vision community, early work focused on prompt learning tech-niques [44], which were later superseded by efficient text-informed linear probes [9,29]. The latter methods provide similar performance while having orders of magnitude less computational complexity [9]. Rapidly, the setting was adopted

For example, as discussed in [31], medical datasets often exhibit highly imbalanced class distri-butions, leading to support sets with underrepresented categories and thereby affecting the final model’s performance in low-shot regimes. In this work, we pro-pose alleviating this issue by using unlabeled data, specifically via pseudo-labels propagated from textual priors. This scenario is appealing since in any data man-agement pipeline, a sufficient amount of unlabeled data is usually assumed to be gathered, and the constraint typically lies in the annotation stage. This principled text-informed linear probe simultaneously learns class prototypes and pseudo-labels from few-shot labeled and unlabeled data via an efficient block-wise optimizer and Optimal Transport. Comprehensive experiments on 12 datasets and 3 modality-specialized medi-cal VLMs demonstrate the benefits of our solver over vanilla few-shot adapters (Fig. 1(b)) to improve predictive performance at lower annotation budgets.

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Setup Modalities, VLMs and transfer tasks. We follow up on [31]: three medical imaging modalities are tackled, for which a pre-trained VLM is adapted to several downstream tasks. We extend the benchmark presented in [31], and incorporate one additional task per modality. For histology , we use CONCH [17], trans-ferred to tissue classification and tumor detection/grading in NCT-CRC [13], SICAPv2 [28], SkinCancer [14], and CCRCC [2]. We employ FLAIR [32] as the ophthalmology foundation model for color fundus images, adapted to dis-ease detection/grading tasks in MESSIDOR [7], MMAC [21], FIVES [12], and mBRSET [20]. Finally, for radiology (chest X-ray), we use CONVIRT [43] with weights from [31], adapted for disease/findings multi-class classification on CheXpert [11], NIH-LT [38,8], COVID [5,23], and PadChest [3]. The number of classes per dataset (C) and their imbalanced ratio (in terms of normalized label-marginal entropy, H(Y ) ∈ (0 , 1) - max indicating a balanced distribution) are presented in Fig 2. We used the text templates proposed by the authors of each VLM in their respective papers/repositories. For specific details on data splits, text prompts, and class names, we refer to our project’s repository: SS-Text-U. 

Baselines. Relevant few-shot solvers are explored. First, gradient-based linear probes are used: vanilla ZS-LP [29], and SoTA text-constrained solutions such as CLAP [29] and LP++ [9,26]. Second, we evaluate so-called training-free so-lutions: Simple-shot (SS) [39], SS-Text [33], and SS-Text+ [31]. For all methods, we stick to the validation-free scenario from [31], and keep the training hyper-parameter fixed across datasets, using the learning schedules detailed in [31]. 

Evaluation protocol. We sample imbalanced, realistic labeled support sets as in [31] with K ∈ { 1, 2, 4, 8, 16 }, as standard in the literature [29,26,31]. Regarding the unlabeled data pool, M = C × 24 additional data points are sampled. Class-wise balanced accuracy (ACA) is employed as a figure of merit, as recommended in [18]. All results are the average across 50 different random seeds for sampling. 

4.2 Results Main results. Figure 1(b) shows the benefit of leveraging unlabeled data via the proposed SS-Text-U solver, which consistently brings average performance gains over all few-shot baselines. Compared to the best training-free solver (SS-Text+), the gains are significant: 10 .9% , 7.1% , 2.7% , 1.3% , and 0.3% in ACA across increasing shots. From a practical standpoint, these numbers translate into a decrease in the required labeling effort of nearly 50% − 75% for K ∈ { 2, 4, 8},e.g., SS-Text-U using 1-shot performs similarly to SS-Text+ when K = 4 .

Efficiency. As shown in Figure 3(a), the proposed solver does not incur sig-nificant computation overhead, being orders of magnitude faster than gradient-based few-shot approaches. ## 5 Discussion 

The proposed SS-Text-U provides consistent gains across most datasets, as shown in Fig. 2. For specific tasks, e.g., NCT, the 1-shot performance is even compara-ble to that in large data regimes. However, we observe that all solvers struggle in a few tasks, where they show moderate performance, e.g., mBRSET. To explore this issue, we use the Silhouette score [25] ( S ∈ (−1, +1) , where 1 is perfect clus-tering and 0 severe overlap) of the embeddings w.r.t. the true labels . Interestingly, Fig 3(d) shows a strong linear correlation ( ρ) between the solver performance and S. This is not the case for zero-shot predictions, which also depend on non-accessible priors of the pre-training data [36]. In some of these difficult tasks, the zero-shot accuracy is exceptionally high. Also, their Silhouette scores closer to S = 0 indicate that the embedding representations and/or label qualities do not suffice to enable well-performing few-shot classifiers. As future work, while we focused on delivering an efficient, well-principled solver using only feature embeddings ( as the baseline linear probes ), other semi-supervised frameworks, e.g., multi-view augmentations and confidence filtering [34], might be explored.
