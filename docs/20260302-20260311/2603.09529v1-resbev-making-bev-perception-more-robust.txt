# RESBev: Making BEV Perception More Robust 

Lifeng Zhuo 1, Kefan Jin 1, Zhe Liu 1, Hesheng Wang 1

Shanghai Jiao Tong University 

Abstract. Bird’s-eye-view (BEV) perception has emerged as a corner-stone of autonomous driving systems, providing a structured, ego-centric representation critical for downstream planning and control. However, real-world deployment faces challenges from sensor degradation and ad-versarial attacks, which can cause severe perceptual anomalies and ulti-mately compromise the safety of autonomous driving systems. To address this, we propose a resilient and plug-and-play BEV perception method (RESBev), which can be easily applied to existing BEV perception meth-ods to enhance their robustness to diverse disturbances. Specifically, we reframe perception robustness as a latent semantic prediction problem. A latent world model is constructed to extract spatiotemporal correla-tions across sequential BEV observations, thereby learning the underly-ing BEV state transitions to predict clean BEV features for reconstruct-ing corrupted observations. The proposed framework operates at the se-mantic feature level of the Lift-Splat-Shoot pipeline, enabling recovery that generalizes across both natural disturbances and adversarial attacks without modifying the underlying backbone. Extensive experiments on the nuScenes dataset demonstrate that with few-shot fine-tuning, RES-Bev significantly improves the robustness of existing BEV perception models against various external disturbances and adversarial attacks. 

Keywords: BEV Perception · Robust Perception · Latent World Model 

## 1 Introduction 

The bird’s-eye-view (BEV) representation has become a foundation of modern autonomous driving perception systems. By transforming multi-camera inputs into a unified top-down representation, it resolves projective ambiguities and provides geometrically consistent scene understanding essential for downstream tasks. Lift-Splat-Shoot (LSS) [15] introduces a foundational framework for this transformation, pioneering camera-centric 3D perception. Building upon this paradigm, numerous BEV perception models have been proposed. For instance, BEVFusion [11] integrates camera features with LiDAR signals to improve geo-metric fidelity and robustness. Meanwhile, BEVFormer [10] leverages temporal self-attention to aggregate BEV features from historical frames, enabling the model to maintain persistent object tracks and handle ego-motion across frames. However, despite impressive performance on benchmarks like nuScenes [1], BEV models, particularly camera-only variants which assume ideal conditions, remain vulnerable. Furthermore, these models are vulnerable to various adversarial attacks, rang-ing from single-step gradient-based perturbations like FGSM [3] to more com-plex, iterative optimization attacks such as PGD [13] and C&W [2]. These attacks introduce minimal input perturbations but lead to significant perfor-mance drops. Benchmarks like RoboBEV [22] have systematically quantified this fragility across various BEV models. While existing robustness strategies provide some solutions, they entail trade-offs. For instance, multi-modal fusion is effective but relies on expensive LiDAR sensors. Similarly, methods like ad-versarial training can only address specific corruptions and fail to generalize to diverse, unpredictable real-world anomalies. The field currently lacks a lightweight and generalizable solution for improv-ing the robustness of existing BEV models. We address this challenge from a new perspective by formulating perception robustness as a temporal prediction problem grounded in latent scene dynamics. Instead of relying solely on in-dividual sensor observations that are easily corrupted, our approach adopts a world-modeling perspective: we model the driving scene as a structured evo-lution of latent states, which provides a stable foundation even when sensor inputs are corrupted. Based on this insight, we introduce RESBev which in-corporates latent world model to learn the causal evolution of BEV states over time (see Fig. 1a). By modeling how BEV states evolve over time, RESBev can reconstruct corrupted features through generative modeling rather than simple temporal aggregation. Extensive experiments demonstrate that RESBev can en-hance the robustness of existing BEV models when facing various corruptions (see Fig. 1b). Our contributions are summarized below: 1. We conduct a systematic analysis of robustness in LSS-based BEV percep-tion pipelines, revealing that effective recovery requires modeling in the BEV semantic space, preserving high-dimensional features before task compres-sion, and leveraging generative temporal prediction. 2. Based on these insights, we propose a plug-and-play model that introduces a latent world model to capture structured temporal dynamics in BEV se-mantic space and generate predictive semantic prior in complex traffic envi-ronments. 3. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed method can effectively enhance the robustness of existing BEV perception methods against various disturbances and improve their generalization to unseen types of perception anomalies. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 BEV Perception 

In autonomous driving, bird’s-eye-view (BEV) representation provides a unified environmental understanding necessary for downstream tasks. Lift-Splat-Shoot (LSS) [15] establishes BEV representation by projecting multi-view 2D image features into a common BEV grid through explicit depth modeling. Building upon this paradigm, numerous BEV perception models have been proposed. For instance, GaussianLSS [17] refines depth estimation by incorporating Gaussian distributions to model uncertainty, while BEVFusion [11] integrates multi-modal sensor data to produce richer BEV representations. FIERY [5] extends the BEV framework to support multiple downstream tasks and leverages multi-frame tem-poral information within a unified architecture. Another line of work adopts Transformer-based architectures. For Example, BEVFormer [10] introduces spa-tiotemporal transformers to aggregate features from historical frames and multi-ple camera views. Despite achieving strong performance on benchmarks such as nuScenes [1], these methods remain vulnerable to various real-world anomalies. 

2.2 Perception Anomalies 

Deep-learning-based perception systems for autonomous driving are highly vul-nerable to various anomalies, including adversarial attacks and natural corrup-tions. Adversarial attacks involve crafting imperceptible perturbations to in-put data that induce incorrect predictions. While classic digital attacks such as FGSM [3], PGD [13], and C&W [2] were initially developed for 2D classifica-tion, recent studies demonstrate their degradation effect on BEV models [24,27]. Beyond these, recent works have introduced targeted attacks for BEV percep-tion, such as semantic perturbations through object swapping [20] and multi-view adversarial attacks like “Fool the Hydra” [19], which exploits vulnerabilities in cross-camera feature alignment. In addition, 3D adversarial examples using techniques like NeRF ( e.g ., Adv3D [8]) create physically plausible perturbations. These attacks lead to perception failures such as missed detections or false posi-tives. Beyond targeted attacks, real-world scenarios present diverse natural cor-ruptions, including adverse weather ( e.g ., fog, dark, snow) and sensor failures (e.g ., camera crash, frame loss). 2.3 Robust Perception 

Ensuring the robustness of perception systems against real-world corruptions such as adverse weather and sensor anomalies, is a critical challenge in au-tonomous driving. As a dominant perception paradigm, bird’s-eye-view (BEV) models inherit these vulnerabilities. To address this, early research [24, 27] fo-cused on analyzing these models to find weak points, revealing that components like view transformers are particularly vulnerable. To standardize the evalu-ation of this problem, benchmarks like RoboBEV [22, 23] were introduced to test models against various anomalies. Consequently, various defense strategies have emerged. These include leveraging complementary data through multi-sensor fusion, where models learn to trust more reliable sensor data in adverse conditions [16] or infer occluded objects from multiple viewpoints [7]. Other approaches ensure temporal consistency by aggregating information from past frames via transformers to maintain stability against sensor failures [10]. Graph-based reasoning methods such as GraphBEV [18] further enhance robustness by modeling structural relationships among BEV entities, enabling the system to propagate contextual information and maintain consistent scene understand-ing even when local observations are corrupted. Despite these advancements, existing defense mechanisms still exhibit significant limitations. For instance, multi-sensor fusion relies on the assumption that complementary sensors remain reliable, which may not hold under severe disturbances. In addition, tempo-ral aggregation often introduces considerable computational overhead and may struggle to handle persistent corruptions. More importantly, most current meth-ods are tailored to handle specific types of corruptions or are tightly coupled to particular architectures. In contrast, our work tackles the fundamental vulner-ability within the transformation process itself, and it can be easily integrated into various LSS-based models as a plug-and-play module, universally enhancing their robustness against a wide range of unpredictable real-world corruptions. 

2.4 World Models in Autonomous Driving 

World model has emerged as a powerful paradigm for capturing complex driv-ing dynamics, categorized into image-based, occupancy-based, and latent-based models. General world models like GAIA-1 [6] and Drive-WM [21] generate fu-ture video sequences for simulation and planning. In the 3D domain, Drive-OccWorld [26] and DriveWorld [14] model 4D occupancy evolution or spatiotem-poral features to enhance safety and detection performance. To balance structure with efficiency, recent works operate in compact latent spaces. LAW [9] integrates a latent dynamics model to refine features for the planning policy, while BEV-World [25] utilizes discrete BEV latents for multimodal scene simulation. ## 3 Analysis 

Robust perception remains a critical challenge in autonomous driving systems, as perception models often fail under real-world anomalies such as adverse weather or sensor failures. To find an effective and resilient solution, we first investigate an important architectural question: At which stage of the Lift-Splat-Shoot (LSS) pipeline should we intervene, and what mechanism is required to reconstruct corrupted features? Our analysis reveals key insights that motivate the design of our framework. 

3.1 Spatial Choice: Image Space vs. BEV Space 

The standard LSS pipeline processes data through three distinct semantic spaces: the 2D perspective image space (Lift), the 2D BEV feature space (Splat), and the low-dimensional task space (Shoot). A critical design choice is whether to predict future states in the image space or the BEV space. As shown in Fig. 2a, features extracted during the Lift stage exhibit severe temporal instability when exposed to continuous environmental noise. Further-more, the perspective view suffers from significant scale and viewpoint shifts during ego-motion, making spatiotemporal dynamics difficult to model. In con-trast, the BEV space (output of the Splat stage) provides a unified, top-down spatial representation. This structure inherently filters out high-frequency 2D vi-sual noise and allows the model to explicitly utilize the ego-vehicle’s kinematics (translation and rotation) to accurately compensate for ego-motion. As a result, the BEV feature sequences maintain high temporal consistency, proving to be an optimal spatial foundation for the latent world model. 3.2 Depth Choice: Semantic Features vs. Task Outputs 

While BEV space is the correct spatial representation, we must further determine whether to intervene after the BEV projection (Splat) or after the task-specific heads (Shoot). We refer to this as the depth choice, driven by the inherent information loss during semantic compression. The Shoot stage compresses high-dimensional, rich semantic BEV features into low-dimensional, task-specific probability distribu-tions ( e.g ., class probabilities for semantic segmentation). As detailed in Tab. 1, attempting to use a latent world model to operate at the Shoot stage results in catastrophic failure during reconstruction. The probabilistic outputs discard essential geometric and visual cues. Once a corruption causes a false prediction at the Shoot stage, the error is irreversible due to the loss of high-dimensional context. Conversely, the Splat stage maintains an uncompressed semantic rep-resentation, providing the necessary spatial and semantic fidelity for the next fusion stage.

3.3 Mechanism Choice: Generative Prior vs. Temporal Aggregation 

We further analyze whether robustness arises from temporal aggregation or from the generative nature of our model. To this end, we compare three variants: a single corrupted frame baseline, a temporal transformer that aggregates features across current corrupted frame and past clean frame, and our latent dynamics world model (LDWM). Results in Tab. 2 show that temporal attention improves performance over the single corrupted frame baseline, indicating that exploit-ing temporal context is beneficial. However, LDWM significantly outperforms the temporal transformer, suggesting that robustness does not primarily stem from aggregating multiple observations but from predicting a temporally con-sistent prior. The limitation of simple feature aggregation becomes apparent when examining the nature of the corruptions. As illustrated in Fig. 2b, ad-versarial perturbations typically introduce only minor feature-level deviations, despite causing severe performance degradation. Because these subtly corrupted features remain numerically close to clean ones, attention mechanisms struggle to filter out the noise. When temporal aggregation fuses the current frame with past observations, the resulting representation inevitably remains polluted by current corrupted frame. In contrast, LDWM predicts the current BEV state directly from historical context, effectively bypassing the corrupted observation to recover a clean, temporally consistent scene representation.

## 4 Method 

4.1 Overall Framework 

Based on the systematic analysis in Sec. 3, we propose RESBev, a framework that treats robust BEV perception as a latent-space temporal prediction and fusion problem. As illustrated in Fig. 3, The core philosophy is to leverage the underlying temporal structure of driving scenes: we construct a Semantic Prior Predictor that models the temporal evolution of the BEV state, learning to pre-dict the current clean BEV features from historical clean frames and ego-motion cues. The predicted state serves as a temporally-grounded prior that is indepen-dent of current sensor corruption. Rather than directly using this prediction, we introduce an Anomaly Reconstructor module that fuses this clean prior with the current fused observation through a query-driven cross-attention mechanism, where the predicted features probe the fused input to selectively extract new valid information while suppressing anomalies. A learnable gating factor adap-tively balances the contributions between the predicted prior and the current observation, enabling the reconstructed output to maintain temporal coherence while remaining responsive to scene changes. 

Semantic Prior Predictor. This module is trained to predict the potential fu-ture state. Given the previous reconstructed feature ( f rec t−1) and the ego-vehicle’s motion, a multi-modal encoder is constructed to extract the semantic informa-tion and capture the dynamic physical law of the traffic scenario. Subsequently, a latent world model is introduced to learn the state transition of the autonomous driving process, which predicts the future BEV prior ( f pred t ) for the anomaly recovery and robust perception. 

Anomaly Reconstructor. This module is trained to recover BEV perception features f rec t that have been corrupted or contain anomalous information. Based on f pred t predicted by the Semantic Prior Predictor, the Anomaly Reconstruc-tor operates at the semantic level to discern the valid information within the current corrupted BEV feature f corrupt t that remains contextually consistent. It further excludes anomalous data where the discrepancy between the predicted and current representations is excessively large. Meanwhile, by leveraging histori-cal information, the module identifies plausible inferences within the predictions. Finally, it integrates these plausible inferences with the portions of the current data that exhibit consistency, thereby recovering the corrupted anomalous infor-mation to achieve robust BEV perception against diverse anomaly conditions. 

4.2 Semantic Prior Predictor 

To restore corrupted observations, we introduce the Semantic Prior Predictor. As illustrated in our pipeline, this module leverages historical information to predict the current clean BEV representations, serving as a clean prior for the subsequent fusion. Rather than computing transitions in the high-dimensional dense feature space, we project the inputs into a compact latent space. Specif-ically, given the reconstructed BEV features f rec t−1 from the previous time step and the corresponding ego-vehicle motion vector at−1 (comprising translation and rotation), we employ a visual encoder Evis and an action encoder Eact to extract discrete visual and action latents. These latents are concatenated to form an action-aware visual latent state. A Transformer-based latent dynamic world model (LDWM) then models the spatiotemporal transition dynamics to predict the future latent state at time t. Finally, a decoder D maps this predicted latent back to the dense BEV feature space to obtain the predicted clean prior f pred t .The entire generation process of this clean prior can be formulated as:

4.3 Anomaly Reconstructor 

While the latent world model provides a clean historical prior, it cannot predict sudden stochastic events, such as a car unexpectedly driving into the scenario. To ground this prediction in real-time reality without absorbing current noise, we introduce a temporal cross attention module. Specifically, given the current multi-view images at time t, which may suffer from various anomalies, we process them through a standard 2D backbone and a view transformer ( e.g ., LSS [15]) to obtain the current corrupted BEV representation, denoted as f corrupt t .To enhance temporal stability and provide a reliable reference for recon-struction, we formulate this process as a query-driven extraction that leverages both current observations and past reconstructed states. Specifically, we concate-nate the previous reconstructed feature f rec t−1 with the current corrupted feature 

f corrupt t to form a comprehensive spatio-temporal representation. The predicted clean feature f pred t acts as the query ( Q) to probe it, which serves as both the Key (K) and Value ( V ). This allows the model to selectively retrieve valid perceptual details from the noisy input while referencing the previous confirmed state to maintain consistency. To integrate this new information without destabilizing the clean prior, we employ a dynamically gated residual connection: 

f rec t = f pred t + α · CrossAttn( f pred t , Concat (f rec t−1, f corrupt t )) , (2) where α ∈ [0 , 1] is a learned dynamic gating factor. This gate adaptively controls the information flow, forcing the model to rely more on the historical prior or the previous reconstruction when the current observation is heavily corrupted, while attending to f corrupt t when it introduces valuable new context.

## 5 Experiments 

This section validates our RESBev model’s effectiveness at enhancing the ro-bustness of LSS-based models. We first measure performance on benchmark cor-ruptions, then assess generalization to unseen corruptions. We follow this with an ablation study analyzing component contributions and performance on con-secutive corruptions. Finally, we provide visualizations of different types of BEV features. 

5.1 Experimental Setup Dataset and Baseline Models. Our experiments are conducted on the widely used nuScenes dataset [1]. We evaluate our method on the task of bird’s-eye-view (BEV) semantic segmentation. To validate the performance of our RESBev model, we integrate it into four LSS-based models: LSS [15], SimpleBEV [4], GaussianLSS [17], and FIERY [5]. To further benchmark our method against state-of-the-art robust perception frameworks, we also compare it with Graph-BEV [18].

5.4 Ablation Study 

To evaluate the contributions of the Semantic Prior Predictor and Anomaly Re-constructor, we conduct an ablation study with results shown in Tab. 5. Using the Semantic Prior Predictor’s prediction directly establishes a strong perfor-mance baseline, confirming the value of a clean temporal prior. ## 6 Conclusion 

To enhance the robustness of LSS-based BEV perception models against vari-ous anomalies, we present RESBev, a novel robust perception framework pow-ered by a latent world model. Unlike traditional methods that rely on simple temporal aggregation, RESBev treats robustness as a predictive reconstruction problem. It incorporates Semantic Prior Predictor to generate a clean temporal prior based on historical context and ego-motion, and Anomaly Reconstructor to selectively integrate this prior with current noisy observations via a query-driven mechanism. Extensive experiments on the nuScenes dataset demonstrate that RESBev significantly enhances the performance of various LSS-based mod-els under diverse corruptions, generalizes effectively to unseen anomalies, and maintains stability during consecutive corruptions. Our work provides a more robust perception foundation, paving the way for reliable navigation in real-world autonomous driving.
