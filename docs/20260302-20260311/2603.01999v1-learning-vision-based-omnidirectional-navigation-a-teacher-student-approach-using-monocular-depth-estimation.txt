# Learning Vision-Based Omnidirectional Navigation: A Teacher-Student Approach Using Monocular Depth Estimation 

Jan Finke 1,2, Wayne Paul Martis 1,2, Adrian Schmelter 1,2,Lars Erbach 1,2, Christian Jestel 1,2, Marvin Wiedemann 1,2

Abstract — Reliable obstacle avoidance in industrial settings demands 3D scene understanding, but widely used 2D LiDAR sensors perceive only a single horizontal slice of the envi-ronment, missing critical obstacles above or below the scan plane. We present a teacher-student framework for vision-based mobile robot navigation that eliminates the need for LiDAR sensors. A teacher policy trained via Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) in NVIDIA Isaac Lab leverages privileged 2D LiDAR observations that account for the full robot footprint to learn robust navigation. The learned behavior is distilled into a student policy that relies solely on monocular depth maps predicted by a fine-tuned Depth Anything V2 model from four RGB cameras. The complete inference pipeline, comprising monocular depth estimation (MDE), policy execu-tion, and motor control, runs entirely onboard an NVIDIA Jetson Orin AGX mounted on a DJI RoboMaster platform, requiring no external computation for inference. In simulation, the student achieves success rates of 82–96.5%, consistently outperforming the standard 2D LiDAR teacher (50–89%). In real-world experiments, the MDE-based student outperforms the 2D LiDAR teacher when navigating around obstacles with complex 3D geometries, such as overhanging structures and low-profile objects, that fall outside the single scan plane of a 2D LiDAR. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) are widely deployed in industrial environments to automate material transport between designated locations. For navigation and collision avoidance, they predominantly rely on 2D Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) sensors [1]. However, since this perception is limited to a single horizontal scan plane, AMRs struggle to operate reliably in complex scenarios involving obstacles that extend outside this plane, such as forklift tines, overhanging loads, or partially occupied shelves [2]. While supplementary 3D sensors such as stereo cameras or time-of-flight systems can mitigate this limitation [3], [4], they add significant cost, weight, and integration complexity compared to standard RGB cameras [5]. Recent advances in monocular depth estimation (MDE), driven by large-scale foundation models, now yield highly accurate relative depth maps from single RGB images with-out dedicated depth sensors [6], [7]. Foundation models such as Depth Anything V2 [8] can infer dense depth maps from single RGB images, effectively turning inexpensive off-the-shelf cameras into depth sensors while preserving rich spatial information. In this work, we combine MDE with deep reinforcement learning (DRL) to realize vision-based navigation, mapping depth observations directly to velocity commands without

A. Deep Reinforcement Learning for Mobile Robot Naviga-tion 

DRL has emerged as a powerful paradigm for learning navigation policies that directly map sensory observations to control commands, bypassing the manual engineering required by traditional layered architectures [13]. However, large-scale real-world evaluations reveal that the choice of sensor modality critically affects sim-to-real transfer [14]. Training such policies requires simulation environments that balance physical realism with computational efficiency [10], [15]. For training, PPO [16] has become the de facto standard for on-policy RL due to its training stability and efficient scaling to massively parallel settings [17]. 

B. Monocular Depth Estimation for Mobile Robots 

LiDAR sensors provide accurate 3D perception for mobile robot navigation but their high cost and power consump-tion [3] motivate the search for lightweight sensor alterna-tives in applications such as intralogistics [1]. While stereo cameras offer an alternative [4], [5], monocular cameras provide the most lightweight solution, requiring only a single lens but depending on learned depth estimation [18], [19]. The advent of deep learning enabled end-to-end depth pre-diction from single images [6], and recent foundation models achieve strong zero-shot generalization across diverse scenes. These models can be categorized by output type: affine-invariant models such as Depth Anything V2 [8] predict relative depth with robust edge preservation, while metric models such as Metric3D v2 [7] recover absolute distances by modeling camera parameters. Lightweight variants enable real-time inference on embedded platforms [20], [21]. Since metrically perfect reconstruction is not required for navi-gation [22], affine-invariant models are particularly suitable for learning-based approaches. Remaining challenges such as scale ambiguity and domain shift [18] motivate robust training strategies for sim-to-real transfer. 

C. Teacher-Student Learning and Sim-to-Real Transfer 

Transferring policies from simulation to real robots re-mains a fundamental challenge. While domain randomiza-tion [11] can partially bridge this gap, it becomes insufficient when deployed sensors differ fundamentally from those used during training. The teacher-student paradigm addresses this by first training a teacher with access to privileged simulation-only information, then distilling its behavior into a student that uses only the sensor modality available during deployment [9]. This approach has been successfully applied across diverse robotic domains including locomotion [12], planetary exploration [23], and vision-based indoor naviga-tion [24]. A major constraint is computational cost: training RL policies on depth maps requires rendering for thousands of environments, demanding substantial GPU memory [17] and limiting the parallelization critical for efficient training. This motivates our approach: the teacher is trained on simulated laser scans with full parallelization, whereas the student is trained to navigate from simulated depth maps, with MDE applied during sim-to-real transfer. III. M ETHODOLOGY 

We propose a teacher-student framework that bridges MDE with RL to enable vision-based robot navigation. The central challenge is the domain gap between rendered and MDE-predicted depth : RL policies trained on ground-truth depth cannot directly consume MDE outputs, which are noisy and partially accurate. Further, the computational over-head of simulating cameras hinders RL policy training. Our framework resolves this through a teacher-student training pipeline. An expert teacher policy trained with privileged 2D LiDAR achieves robust navigation via parallel simula-tion [17], then distills its behavior offline into a student that operates on noise-augmented depth maps. Domain random-ization [11] and depth augmentation facilitate sim-to-real transfer. Section III-A formalizes the navigation problem. Section III-B describes the MDE module. A. Problem Formulation 

We address collision-free goal-directed navigation for an omnidirectional mobile robot in indoor environments with static obstacles, formulated as a partially observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) (S, A, O, R, P, γ ) with state space S, action space A, observation space O, reward func-tion R, transition dynamics P , and discount factor γ ∈ [0 , 1) .The robot receives partial observations ot ∈ O of the true state st ∈ S and selects actions at ∈ A according to a policy πθ (at | ot). Crucially, the observation space differs between teacher and student: the teacher observes privileged 2D LiDAR scans (Section III-C), while the student receives monocular depth maps (Section III-D). Both policies share the action space and proprioceptive observations, but only the teacher requires the reward function, as the student is trained offline via behavior cloning. 

B. Monocular Depth Estimation Backbone 

As established in Section II-B, affine-invariant depth mod-els are sufficient for learning-based navigation. We select Depth Anything V2 [8] in its Vision Transformer (ViT)-Small variant (25M parameters) for its zero-shot generaliza-tion and compatibility with embedded inference on NVIDIA Jetson platforms. To reduce the domain gap between ren-dered and MDE-predicted depth, we fine-tune the model on domain-specific metric data from a warehouse environment. 

Fine-Tuning of Depth Anything V2: For fine-tuning, we recorded a domain-specific dataset of 24,499 RGB and depth map pairs using an Orbbec Femto Bolt time-of-flight (ToF) camera (Fig. 3), covering typical logistics scenarios such as loaded pallets, industrial trucks, and high-bay warehouses. The ViT-Small model from Depth Anything V2 was then fine-tuned for 80 epochs on the training split (77.6%). Fine-tuning resulted in an improvement of 118.4% in the 

δ1 accuracy on the test data split compared to the metric ViT-Small model from Depth Anything V2, which was fine-tuned exclusively on the Hypersim dataset [25]. Here, δ1 denotes the fraction of pixels satisfying max( d∗/d, d/d ∗) < 1.25 ,where d and d∗ are the predicted and ground-truth depth, respectively.

We evaluate our framework in simulation and on the real robot platform. 

A. Isaac Lab Simulation Environment 

Training is conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU with 24 GB VRAM. The teacher policy is trained in Isaac Sim 5.1 with the Isaac Lab framework, running 1024 parallel environments for large-scale RL (Fig. 1). The training uses three arena configurations of varying size to encourage generalization across different spatial scales. In each episode, obstacles sampled from a pool of 15 distinct object types are randomly placed within the arena to create diverse navigation scenarios. The student policy is subse-quently trained offline on the same GPU using the logged demonstration data from the teacher (see Section III-D). 

B. Hardware Platform and Camera Calibration 

For our experiments, we utilize the DJI RoboMaster S1, which features four Mecanum wheels for omnidirectional movement. The robot is equipped with an NVIDIA Jetson Orin AGX, which is used for onboard inference of the student policy and to run the MDE model locally. To have a full view of the robot’s surroundings, the robot is equipped with four RGB cameras with AR0234 global shutter image sensors. These sensors are connected to a Luxonis FFC4 module, which generates a hardware trigger to synchronize all cam-eras and outputs images at a resolution of 480 × 300 pixels. The sensors are equipped with Arducam LN056 wide-angle lenses. All cameras are calibrated to each other using the Basalt framework [27] and an AprilTag [28] calibration target. To undistort the cameras, we upload the Kannala-Brandt calibration parameters [29] to the Luxonis boards, yielding a FOV of 93 .97 ◦ × 67 .64 ◦ for each camera. Additionally, an RPLIDAR S2 2D LiDAR sensor is mounted on top of the robot, providing 360 ◦ range mea-surements. Because the angular offset of the raw scan varies between sweeps, the measurements are rebinned into 

nscan = 3000 fixed angular positions to match the simulation raycasting layout. This enables deployment of the teacher policy on the real robot for baseline comparison with the MDE-based student policy. Fig. 7: DJI RoboMaster platform: (a) simulation model, (b) real robot, (c) Mecanum wheel with sphere colliders, and (d) detailed collider geometry of a single Mecanum roller. 

C. Simulation Asset 

The simulation asset is shown in Fig. 7. The robot model consists of four Mecanum wheels, each with 12 free-rolling rollers. Following the approach of [30], each roller is approx-imated by 7 sphere colliders. The physical parameters of the asset, such as friction and joint damping, are optimized using recorded ground-truth motion data and the system identifi-cation approach from [30], [31]. Additionally, a deadzone is applied to each wheel to account for the inability of the RoboMaster’s motors to actuate at low velocities. 

D. Simulation Results: Teacher Policy 

The teacher policy is trained for 300 epochs, completing in approximately 120 minutes. Fig. 8 shows the average episode reward over training. The reward increases steeply within the first 25 epochs and plateaus at approximately 14.5 after epoch 50, indicating fast and stable convergence of the teacher policy. Fig. 9 illustrates the episode termination distribution: the policy transitions from timeouts through a collision-heavy phase to mostly successful episodes, reaching a peak success rate of 98.5%, noting that training episodes sample a higher and variable number of objects than the fixed evaluation configurations in Table IV.

F. Real-World Results 

To evaluate sim-to-real transfer, we deploy both policies on the real robot in an 8 m × 8 m arena containing objects of varying size, as shown in Fig. 10. Robot trajectories are recorded using a VICON motion capture system, which provides ground-truth goal distance and direction to both policies, isolating the exteroceptive perception (2D LiDAR vs. MDE) as the sole variable under evaluation. For this scenario, we measured the success rate of the teacher and student policies deployed on the RoboMaster for three different endpoints from a common starting point, with 10 runs per endpoint. Table V shows the success rates of the real-world experi-ments. The MDE-based student consistently outperforms the LiDAR-based teacher across all three endpoints, achieving an average success rate of 80% compared to 37% for the teacher. The teacher’s failures are predominantly caused by collisions with low-lying obstacles outside the LiDAR scan plane, which the depth-based student perceives through its full-frame FOV. G. Sim-to-Real Analysis 

Fig. 11 shows example trajectories for both policies during real-world deployment. Two of the three teacher runs collide with the ladder, while the student reaches the goal. However, the student trajectories exhibit oscillatory behavior. These oscillations are attributed to a combination of inference latency, residual MDE depth errors, and inaccuracies in the dynamic modeling of the real robot. Despite fine-tuning the Depth Anything V2 model on domain-specific data, a mismatch in absolute distance re-mains, as shown in Fig. 12. For visualization, the figure displays a point cloud generated from the MDE depth maps using the camera calibration parameters from Section IV-B, alongside the absolute range measurements from the RPLIDAR S2 (shown in red). The MDE model estimates close-range distances with higher accuracy, while errors increase at larger distances, resulting in a residual sim-to-real gap. V. C ONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK
