# Look Twice: Training-Free Evidence Highlighting in Multimodal Large Language Models 

Marco Morini, Sara Sarto, Marcella Cornia, and Lorenzo Baraldi 

University of Modena and Reggio Emilia, Italy 

{name.surname}@unimore.it 

Abstract. Answering questions about images often requires combining visual understanding with external knowledge. Multimodal Large Lan-guage Models (MLLMs) provide a natural framework for this setting, but they often struggle to identify the most relevant visual and textual evidence when answering knowledge-intensive queries. In such scenarios, models must integrate visual cues with retrieved textual evidence that is often noisy or only partially relevant, while also localizing fine-grained visual information in the image. In this work, we introduce Look Twice (LoT), a training-free inference-time framework that improves how pre-trained MLLMs utilize multimodal evidence. Specifically, we exploit the model attention patterns to estimate which visual regions and retrieved textual elements are relevant to a query, and then generate the answer conditioned on this highlighted evidence. The selected cues are high-lighted through lightweight prompt-level markers that encourage the model to re-attend to the relevant evidence during generation. Experi-ments across multiple knowledge-based VQA benchmarks show consistent improvements over zero-shot MLLMs. Additional evaluations on vision-centric and hallucination-oriented benchmarks further demonstrate that visual evidence highlighting alone improves model performance in settings without textual context, all without additional training or architectural modifications. Source code will be publicly released. 

Keywords: Knowledge-based VQA · Multimodal · Vision-and-Language 

## 1 Introduction 

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) [2, 4, 23, 45] have rapidly emerged as a powerful framework for vision-language reasoning, enabling systems that interpret images and answer natural language queries through a unified genera-tive interface. By combining LLMs [59] with visual encoders [33, 39, 57], these systems can jointly process textual and visual inputs, supporting a wide range of applications including visual dialogue and open-ended question answering. A particularly challenging scenario arises when answering a question requires information that is not directly observable in the image. Many real-world queries depend on external knowledge, domain-specific facts, or contextual information that can not be reliably stored in the model parameters. Knowledge-based Visual

Question Answering (KB-VQA) addresses this challenge by augmenting the model input with passages retrieved from external knowledge sources [7, 30]. In this retrieval-augmented setting, the model must reason jointly over the visual input and the retrieved textual elements to generate the final answer. However, retrieval is inherently imperfect, and the resulting context often contains partially relevant or noisy passages [5]. When such evidence is appended to the prompt, the model may struggle to identify which pieces of information actually support the answer, leading to degraded reasoning. Prior works have addressed this challenge by designing increasingly sophisticated retrieval-augmented generation pipelines, incorporating re-ranking [51,52], filtering [14,53], or additional reasoning modules [9, 10]. While effective, these approaches typically rely on multi-stage processing and often require additional training or task-specific components, introducing significant computational overhead and system complexity. Moreover, fine-tuning for such pipelines may encourage it to adhere to task-specific formats or reasoning patterns, potentially limiting their generalization ability. Beyond textual noise, the visual modality introduces complementary chal-lenges. Many KB-VQA questions rely on evidence localized in specific image regions, yet MLLMs often rely on coarse global cues rather than precise spa-tial grounding [3, 12, 44]. Moreover, attention mechanisms in large Transformer architectures can exhibit attention sinks [13, 50], where a small subset of to-kens consistently attracts disproportionate attention mass regardless of semantic relevance. Similar effects have been observed in vision-language models, where visually salient but uninformative patches can dominate attention [19], weakening the model ability to focus on the most informative visual evidence. In this work, we study how pretrained MLLMs exploit multimodal evidence at inference time, using KB-VQA as a principled evaluation setting. Rather than modifying the retrieval pipeline or fine-tuning the model, we investigate whether the model internal attention dynamics can be leveraged to better guide reasoning over multimodal inputs. To this end, we introduce Look Twice (LoT) , a training-free inference-time framework that improves multimodal evidence selection by explicitly highlighting query-relevant cues in both retrieved text and the input image. The key idea is to let the model look twice at the input: a first lightweight generation step produces a single token, which is used to estimate which visual regions and textual elements are relevant to the query, while a second generation pass produces the final answer conditioned on this highlighted evidence. Specifi-cally, LoT extracts attention signals from the first step to identify relevant visual regions and context tokens. On the visual side, we aggregate object-conditioned attention between question and visual tokens to obtain a query-specific relevance map, and suppress spurious activations caused by attention sinks through a filtering step that mitigates their impact during visual evidence localization while leaving the model unchanged. On the textual side, we leverage attention from the generated token to identify informative sentences within the retrieved context. The selected textual sentences and localized image region are then highlighted through lightweight prompt-level markers, encouraging the model to re-attend to these cues during answer generation without modifying its parameters. We evaluate LoT on multiple KB-VQA benchmarks [7, 21, 30] across a diverse set of off-the-shelf MLLMs in a zero-shot setting, consistently improving perfor-mance over the corresponding base models. Furthermore, we conduct additional experiments on standard vision-centric [38] and hallucination-oriented [22, 42] benchmarks where no textual context is provided, showing that the proposed visual evidence highlighting alone improves model performance. These results indicate that leveraging internal attention signals provides a simple yet effective mechanism for improving evidence selection in MLLMs. 

Contributions. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 

– We introduce Look Twice (LoT), a training-free inference-time framework that improves multimodal evidence selection in pretrained MLLMs by explicitly highlighting query-relevant cues in both retrieved text and input image. 

– We propose a two-pass inference strategy that leverages the model internal attention dynamics to first identify relevant visual regions and textual evi-dence, and then guide answer generation through lightweight prompt-level highlighting, without modifying model parameters or retrieval pipelines. 

– Notably, our look-twice strategy introduces only negligible computational overhead, as it requires generating just a single additional token at the beginning to analyze the model attention patterns. 

– We demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple KB-VQA bench-marks with diverse off-the-shelf MLLMs in a zero-shot setting, and show that visual evidence highlighting alone improves performance on vision-centric and hallucination-oriented benchmarks. 

## 2 Related Work 

Knowledge-Based VQA. Recent MLLMs have demonstrated strong capabilities in jointly reasoning over images and text, enabling progress on tasks such as multimodal dialogue and cross-modal reasoning [2,4,23,38,45]. When answering a question requires information beyond what is directly observable in the image, the task is commonly referred to as knowledge-based VQA (KB-VQA). In this setting, models must integrate visual understanding with external knowledge to generate answers grounded in both modalities. Early benchmarks [28, 34] introduced the need for external reasoning but remain limited in scale and complexity, while more recent datasets such as Encyclopedic-VQA [30] and InfoSeek [7] increase difficulty by requiring fine-grained, entity-centric reasoning over large-scale knowledge sources. To address these challenges, many recent approaches rely on sophisticated retrieval-augmented pipelines, additional supervision, or task-specific training to better integrate external evidence [5, 9, 10, 14, 51, 52, 56]. In contrast, we use KB-VQA as a principled evaluation setting to study how pretrained MLLMs utilize retrieved textual context and visual evidence at inference time. By applying our training-free evidence highlighting strategy to strong off-the-shelf models, we isolate improvements in multimodal evidence selection without introducing additional supervision, architectural changes, or parameter updates. 4 M. Attention Dynamics in Vision-Language Models. Understanding how multimodal models allocate attention across textual and visual inputs is cen-tral to interpreting their reasoning behavior. Prior studies show that attention distributions in large models exhibit structured patterns [8, 20, 41], motivating analyses of sparsity, head specialization, and redundancy [11, 31]. More recent work highlights the phenomenon of attention sinks , where a small subset of tokens or image regions captures a disproportionate amount of attention while other potentially relevant elements are ignored. In LLMs, such sinks often cor-respond to special or punctuation tokens and can hinder effective long-context reasoning [13, 50, 55]. Similar behaviors have been observed in vision-language models, where background or visually salient patches dominate attention despite contributing little to the final prediction [19, 47]. Together, these analyses suggest that attention concentration is not solely data-driven, but also influenced by systematic biases in the attention mechanism. In multimodal reasoning and KB-VQA, where answers require integrating textual and visual cues, such imbalances can lead the model to over-rely on superficially salient tokens or regions while overlooking query-relevant evidence. 

Inference - Level Attention Analysis. Beyond analyzing attention concen-tration effects, a growing line of work explores modifying attention maps at inference time to improve grounding and reduce hallucinations in large models. In LLMs, inference-time methods have used attention scores to identify relevant textual evidence without additional training [26]. In multimodal settings, some approaches identify dominant visual tokens and redistribute attention toward more informative regions [19]. Others leverage internal attention dynamics to enhance semantically central visual regions during generation [27], suppress dis-ruptive instruction tokens that distort cross-modal alignment [6], or reinforce high-focus attention patterns across heads to strengthen visual conditioning [58]. Additional methods derive saliency signals from attention shifts and amplify relevant visual features at inference time [32]. Our approach follows this inference-time paradigm but focuses on improving multimodal evidence selection. We treat the model internal attention as an implicit relevance signal to identify query-relevant textual tokens and visual regions, which are then explicitly highlighted through lightweight prompt-level markers. This strategy guides the model to re-attend to the most informative cues during generation, without modifying model parameters or architecture.

3.1 Preliminaries Task Definition. We consider a pretrained MLLM that processes an image together with a textual prompt and generates answers autoregressively. Given an input image XV ∈ RH×W ×3 and a textual question XT = [ x1, . . . , x NT ], the model first converts the image into a sequence of visual tokens using its vision encoder and multimodal projector. This produces NV visual tokens corresponding to spatial regions of the image. In the KB-VQA setting, the model additionally receives retrieved textual evidence. A retrieval module selects the top-n textual passages from a knowledge base ( e.g. , composed of Wikipedia documents), which are concatenated into a context sequence XC = [ c1, . . . , c NC ].The model processes a single multimodal sequence obtained by concatenating visual tokens, question tokens, and retrieved context, X = [ XV ; XT ; XC ] with total length S = NV + NT + NC . During inference, the model generates an answer autoregressively conditioned on this sequence.

Our approach operates by extracting attention signals from the pretrained MLLM, filtering spurious activations caused by attention sinks, and highlighting the resulting evidence through lightweight prompt markers at inference time.

Our primary evaluation is conducted on Encyclopedic-VQA (E-VQA) [30] and InfoSeek [7], two large-scale datasets that require reasoning over Wikipedia entities. In addition, we report results on two complementary entity-centric benchmarks, ViQuAE [21] and OVEN [15]. Standard MLLM Evaluation Benchmarks. To evaluate LoT beyond KB-VQA, we additionally consider standard MLLM benchmarks covering visual reasoning, OCR, and hallucination robustness. Specifically, we report results on vision-centric benchmarks such as RealWorldQA [49] and V-Star [48], as well as OCR- and chart-focused datasets including TextVQA [36], ChartQA [29], and OCRBench [25]. We further assess hallucination robustness using POPE [22] and AMBER [42], which measure whether model outputs remain faithful to the visual content. Except for AMBER, for which we use the official evaluation pipeline 2,all other benchmarks are evaluated using the evaluation suite of [38]. Together, these benchmarks provide a complementary assessment of visual grounding and robustness in the absence of external knowledge retrieval. 

4.2 Implementation Details Models and Baselines. We evaluate our method on a diverse set of recent MLLMs with different capacities. Specifically, we consider Qwen2-VL [43] (2B, 7B), Qwen2.5-VL [2] (3B, 7B, 32B), Qwen3-VL [1] (4B, 8B), and Intern3.5-VL [45] (4B, 8B, 38B). These models span multiple architectures and parameter scales, providing a broad set of off-the-shelf models for evaluation. 

Retrieval Pipeline. In the KB-VQA experiments, we adopt a cross-modal entity retrieval pipeline to obtain relevant knowledge for a given input image. Following [52], each Wikipedia entity of the knowledge base is represented by a concise textual summary. All entity summaries are embedded using the textual encoder of EVA-CLIP [37]. Given an input image, we encode it using the visual encoder and perform image-to-text similarity search via inner-product matching using FAISS [17]. The top-n retrieved entities, with n = 3 in our experiments, are concatenated to form the textual context provided to the MLLM.

During the generation of the first token, we analyze attention patterns across different decoder layer ranges, selecting modality-specific subsets corresponding to where MLLMs typically process each modality. Prior work shows that deeper layers rely more heavily on textual cues when producing the final answer, whereas visual grounding signals tend to emerge in intermediate layers [16, 18, 26]. Attention sinks, in contrast, appear throughout the network. For textual highlighting, we select the evidence sentence whose attention score is equal to the maximum among all sentences. Finally, in the bounding box extraction step (cf. Eq. 5) we set β = 2 .

## 5 Conclusion 

We introduced LoT, a training-free inference-time framework that improves mul-timodal evidence selection in pretrained MLLMs by highlighting query-relevant cues in retrieved text and input images. By leveraging the model internal at-tention signals, LoT identifies relevant textual and visual evidence and guides answer generation without modifying model parameters. Experiments on multiple KB-VQA benchmarks show consistent improvements across architectures in a zero-shot setting. We further demonstrate that visual highlighting alone benefits standard MLLM benchmarks, suggesting that improved visual grounding can enhance multimodal reasoning more broadly. ## A Additional Details on KB-VQA Benchmarks 

In the context of KB-VQA, models are required to answer questions by retrieving and leveraging information from an external knowledge base, typically composed of structured or unstructured resources such as Wikipedia. We evaluate our approach on several representative KB-VQA datasets, all of which link images and questions to Wikipedia entities and provide corresponding knowledge bases. 

Encyclopedic-VQA [30]. This dataset contains 221k question-answer pairs, each associated with up to five images and covering 16.7k fine-grained entities. Questions are divided into single-hop and two-hop types: single-hop questions can be answered using information from a single Wikipedia page, whereas two-hop questions require sequential retrieval across multiple pages. The dataset is divided into training, validation, and test splits comprising 1M, 13.6k, and 5.8k samples. All experiments are conducted on the test split, which includes 4.8k single-hop questions. The dataset also provides an external knowledge base derived from Wikipedia, comprising about 2M pages, each including the article title, textual content, and associated images. In our experiments, we employ the original 2M-page knowledge base provided with the dataset. 

InfoSeek [7]. It contains approximately 1.3M image-question-answer triplets associated with about 11k unique Wikipedia entities. The dataset is divided into training, validation, and test splits, comprising roughly 934k, 73k, and 348k samples, respectively. Both validation and test sets include questions about unseen entities. InfoSeek also provides an external knowledge base covering approximately 6M Wikipedia entities. Following previous works [5,9], experiments are conducted using a knowledge base of 100k pages and employing 3-shot examples in the prompt to align the response with the dataset format. 

OVEN [15]. This dataset is designed to link an image and a textual query to the corresponding Wikipedia entity, covering approximately 6M entities. OVEN includes nearly 5M training examples, along with validation and test splits, as well as a human-verified evaluation set. Attention Map Filtering. Fig. 5 presents qualitative examples illustrating the effect of the attention map filtering. As shown, the raw attention maps contain several activations scattered across the image and far from the target object, making the extraction of a reliable bounding box difficult. After removing attention sinks, the resulting attention maps become significantly more concen-trated around the target object, leading to bounding boxes (shown in red) that accurately localize the queried object in the scene. 

Bounding Box Extraction. To extract multimodal evidence from the model, we experiment with several strategies to aggregate filtered attention maps and derive bounding boxes that localize the relevant visual regions. We consider three main bounding box extraction methods: 

– Min-Max Coordinates: This approach considers all non-zero values in the attention map and computes the bounding box by taking the minimum and maximum coordinates along each axis. 

– Morphological Filtering: The attention map is first thresholded over 0.1 to create a binary map. Morphological closing operations (dilation followed by erosion) are then applied to obtain a connected region, and the bounding box is computed around it. 

– Weighted Centroid: This method computes the centroid of the attention map weighted by the attention values themselves. The bounding box is then defined around the centroid using the standard deviation of the attention distribution along each axis, scaled by a multiplier to control box size. To quantitatively evaluate these approaches, we compare the extracted bound-ing boxes with predictions from GroundingDINO [24] (using the Base version), an open-vocabulary object detector which serves as a proxy groundtruth due to the absence of bounding box annotations for the queried objects. For this analysis, all evaluations are conducted on E-VQA. Table 4 reports results across standard metrics: Intersection over Union (IoU), Coverage, Precision, and Center Distance. As shown, the weighted centroid method consistently achieves the best coverage and IoU scores combination, indicating that it captures the most relevant regions of the attention map while remaining tightly localized around the target object. The min-max method produces overly large and sparse bounding boxes, leading to lower precision, while the morphological approach generates compact regions but occasionally fails to cover all relevant attention peaks. Overall, the weighted centroid method provides the best trade-off between accuracy and compactness, making it the most effective strategy for bounding box extraction. 

Token Statistics. LoT requires generating a single extra token at the beginning and performs a lightweight post-processing step on the extracted attention maps. Look Twice: Training-Free Evidence Highlighting in Multimodal LLMs 21 

Importantly, the entire procedure operates in a fully zero-shot setting and does not require any additional training or model fine-tuning. To quantify the impact of these operations on E-VQA with Qwen2.5-VL-3B, we measure the average number of input and generated tokens. Thanks to the bounding box extraction step, LoT reduces the number of visual tokens from 291 to 208 on average (a 28.5% reduction), allowing the model to focus more on the main subject of the question. Moreover, the model generates ∼18 tokens on average to produce the final answer, meaning that the additional token required by the first pass increases the overall generation cost by only ∼5.6%.

## C Additional Experimental Results 

Comparison with Different Visual Highlighting Strategies. Table 5 compares several visual highlighting strategies on E-VQA [30] and InfoSeek [7], analyzing their impact on Qwen2.5-VL-3B and InternVL3.5-4B. First, we test simply drawing the computed bounding box on the input image, which reduces performance by roughly 2 points on E-VQA. Another strategy involves cropping the image to the bounding box and feeding only this region during the second-generation step. We also evaluate using both the full image and the cropped region Look Twice: Training-Free Evidence Highlighting in Multimodal LLMs 23 

without any highlighting, as well as adding highlighting markers to both the original image and the crop. Overall, the most effective strategy is to apply markers solely on the cropped bounding box while excluding the original image. This approach not only achieves the highest performance but also reduces the number of visual tokens processed, thanks to the models dynamic visual processing. 

Comparison with Different Textual Highlighting Strategies. Table 6, in-stead, compares different textual highlighting strategies on the same datasets and using the same MLLMs. In the first strategy, we add highlighting markers across the entire retrieved context, yielding a modest improvement over the baseline that uses the raw retrieved content. We also study the effect of granularity. LoT highlights the most relevant sentences ( i.e. , sentence-level), while we experiment with highlighting entire passages ( i.e. , passage-level). On E-VQA, sentence-level consistently outperforms passage-level, while on InfoSeek passage-level shows a slight drop in performance. Importantly, both strategies use the same attention maps extracted with LoT, showing that different ways of leveraging these signals still improve reasoning over the retrieved text.
