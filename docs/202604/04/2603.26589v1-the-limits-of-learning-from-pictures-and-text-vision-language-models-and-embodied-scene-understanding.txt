# Abstract 

What information is sufficient to learn the full richness of human scene understand -

ing? The distributional hypothesis holds that the statistical co -occurrence of language 

and images captures the conceptual knowledge underlying visual cognition. Vision -

language models (VLMs) are trained on massive paired text -image corpora but lack 

embodied experience, making them an ideal test of the distributional hypothesis . We 

report two experiments comparing descriptions generated by 18 VLMs to those of 

over 2000 human observers across 15 high -level scene understanding tasks, spanning 

general knowledge, affordances, sensory experiences, affective responses, and future 

prediction. Because many tasks lack ground truth answers, we developed a Human -

Calibrated Cosine Distance (HCD) metric that measures VLM output similarity to 

the distribution of human responses, scaled by within -human variability. In Experi -

ment 1, VLMs approached human -level performance on general knowledge tasks, but 

showed a robust deficit for affordance tasks that resisted prompt engineering and did 

not improve with newer model releases. In Experiment 2, we tested six mechanistic hy -

potheses for explaining this affordance gap, finding that the deficit was structural rather 

than stylistic and was not resolved by providing explicit spatial information. Corpus 

analyses revealed that image captioning datasets contain sparse agent -addressed af -

fordance language, consistent with Gricean accounts of why embodied knowledge may 

be systematically underrepresented in language. Together, these findings suggest that 

distributional learning from images and text is insufficient for affordance -based scene 

understanding, implying that some dimensions of human visual cognition may require 

the kind of agent -centered, three -dimensional experience that no photograph or caption 

can encode. 2

# Introduction 

Consider the photograph in the left -hand column of Figure 1. Immediately and effort -

lessly, you can name its general category (a thrift store), and name some of the objects 

in it (the American flag, or vinyl records, for example). Although categorization and 

object identification are considered “core” components of human (DiCarlo, Zoccolan, &

Rust, 2012 ) and machine (Russakovsky et al., 2015 ) visual understanding, human scene 

understanding is characterized by a deep inferential chain of thought that allows us to 

reason about what actions could be taken in the scene, what it would feel like to be in 

the scene, predict what will happen next, and speculate about what happened before. 

Where did this knowledge come from? What information is sufficient to develop these 

abilities? Recently -developed vision -language models (VLMs) provide a method for 

testing these questions. VLMs are trained with a massive amount of language content 

and photographs, but they are not embodied, nor do they have the capacity to explore 

the world to discover its causal connections. Therefore, if a VLM is able to succeed in 

high -level scene understanding tasks, we can infer that those tasks can be learned from 

the statistical structure of language and images. The answers to these questions serve 

both a basic question about human cognition (what are the necessary inputs for scene 

understanding?), as well as a practical one (can VLMs serve as models of human scene 

perception?). On the practical side, VLMs are already being used in psychological sci -

ences to scale cognitive research by annotating items such as ground truth categories 

(Rong, Gifford, Düzel, & Cichy, 2025 ), emotional features like facial expressions (Niu, 

El -Tawil, Romana, & Provost, 2025 ), and semantic descriptions ( Murlidaran & Eck -

stein, 2025 ). As use of VLMs as a modelling tool increases, it is necessary to assess 

the quality of this model generated content. 

The distributional hypothesis posits that language captures an impressive amount of 

human conceptual knowledge, and predicts that much of high -level scene knowledge 

should be recoverable from the statistical structure of language and images alone. A

strong version of this hypothesis would predict high performance across a range of scene 

understanding tasks. There are reasons to take the distributional hypothesis seriously. 

For example, blind individuals share color category structure with sighted individuals, 

despite never visually experiencing color (Kim, Aheimer, Montané Manrara, & Bedny, 

2021 ), and VLMs share similar performance patterns on sensory concepts with and 

without visual training ( Marjieh, Sucholutsky, van Rijn, Jacoby, & Griffiths, 2024 ), 

suggesting that at least some visual concepts can be learned without visual experi -

ence. Additionally, even higher -level cognitive abilities, such as theory of mind, seem 

to be largely captured by a massive amount of language training ( Trott, Jones, Chang, 

Michaelov, & Bergen, 2023 ). But there is also reason for skepticism of the distribu -

tional hypothesis — many large language models (LLMs) improve with visual concept 

grounding ( Xu et al., 2025 ), showing that sensory experience augments the informa -

tion provided by language, and suggests that other grounding may lead to further 

improvements. Further, VLMs demonstrate persistent deficits in some basic visual 3

tasks ( Tangtartharakul & Storrs, 2026 ; Tong et al., 2024 ), so it may be the case that 

even massive -scale learning of two -dimensional visual inputs can never fully generalize 

to the three dimensional world. A second possible issue is in the pragmatics of hu -

man communication. Humans tend not to express ideas that are self -evident to other 

humans (Grice, 1991 ). Therefore, if LLMs and VLMs are trained on human communi -

cation patterns that systematically compress information that is obvious to embodied 

agents, no amount of text -image training would allow a VLM to learn about certain 

tasks. Finally, empirical evidence shows that both typically -developing children and 

children who have had their sight surgically restored use self -motion to parse and seg -

ment objects ( Ostrovsky, Meyers, Ganesh, Mathur, & Sinha, 2009 ; Soska, Adolph, & 

Johnson, 2010 ), suggesting that humans may require three -dimensional experience to 

solve the problem of object segmentation. 

In this work, we tested the distributional hypothesis across a broad range of high -level 

scene understanding tasks. These included core, general knowledge tasks, such as cat -

egorization and general scene descriptions, as well as affordances, sensory experiences, 

affective experiences, and future forecasting. Critically, these task classes differ in the 

degree to which they depend on embodied or agent -centered knowledge, yet human ob -

servers have been shown to perform these tasks with scenes quickly and easily. Thus, 

this work differentiates itself from traditional benchmarking in two ways: first by ex -

amining patterns of performance across tasks to determine what makes a task easy 

versus hard for VLMs, and second, by selecting tasks that go beyond information that 

is grounded in objects, attributes, and relations and towards those that require predic -

tive, counterfactual, and inferential knowledge. A natural consequence of using these 

types of tasks is the need to define performance in a human -centered way. Because 

some tasks have no ground truth, we cannot evaluate VLMs using standard accuracy 

metrics. Instead, we assess whether VLM outputs fall within the distribution of human 

responses, suggesting that the models reason about scenes in a human -like way. 

This paper is divided into two experiments. In Experiment 1, we compare descrip -

tions from 18 VLMs to human descriptions across 15 high -level scene understanding 

tasks, arranged hierarchically in five task types. We developed a method for estab -

lishing whether a VL model response is within the human noise ceiling. Therefore, 

VLM success is defined within quantitative similarity to the human distribution of re -

sponses. We examined how robust the task -specific patterns are to changes in factors 

such as prompt, model family, and embedding type. In Experiment 2, we narrow our 

investigation to explore a key finding from Experiment 1 that there is a persistent gap 

between VLM descriptions and human descriptions for affordance tasks. We evaluate 

six hypotheses for why these differences persist for these specific sets of tasks. We find 

that VLMs approach human -level performance on general knowledge tasks but show a 

persistent deficit for affordance tasks that resists prompt engineering, does not improve 

with newer models, and reflects structural rather than superficial differences. 4

# Experiment 1: Human and VLM Scene Descriptions 

The goal of Experiment 1 was to assess whether VLMs demonstrate human -like visual 

scene understanding across a range of high -level tasks. If VLMs are successful at a 

task, then the task is learnable from the correspondence of distributional semantics 

and two -dimensional photographs. Many high -level tasks require counterfactual and 

causal reasoning, or invoke subjective qualitative experience, and thus do not have 

ground truth answers. To quantify VLM similarity to the distribution of human de -

scriptions, we created a human -calibrated cosine distance metric. Throughout, we 

assessed the robustness of task related differences across a number of factors such as 

prompt type, model type, and embedding type. Our central finding of this experiment 

is that affordance tasks consistently had the least similarity between VLM and human 

descriptions, even across prompt types and time. 

Our first step was to obtain human and VL model scene descriptions across a diverse 

set of scene understanding tasks and to quantitatively compare their similarities and 

differences. 

# Methods 

Image Selection 

We selected a set of 40 images of real -world scenes spanning indoor, outdoor, natural, 

urban, public, and private spaces. This sample size balances the need for sufficient sta -

tistical power with the practical constraints of collecting detailed descriptions across 

15 different tasks, 18 different VLMs, 10 human observers, and three prompting con -

ditions, for a total of over 3.2 million different descriptions. Photographs were selected 

from an internal lab dataset that has not yet been released to the public. As such, 

these photographs constitute new content not present in the training corpora used by 

any model. This allows us to conduct a stronger test of how well models can generalize 

to new content. Images depicted quotidian real -world settings that do not reflect pro -

totypical scene categories, limiting the extent to which models could rely on learned 

category -text associations. 

Tasks 

Our goal was to create a battery of high -level scene understanding tasks that range 

from objective and knowledge -based to predictive and embodied, capturing the highly 

inferential and multi -modal nature of scene understanding. Our battery consisted of 

15 tasks that were divided into five groups: general knowledge, affordances, sensory 

experiences, affective experiences, and future casting. We will introduce each group in 

turn. 5

The general knowledge group represents objective facts about the scene’s category, 

objects, and basic layout. These tasks were chosen to reflect what some have termed 

“core” human visual cognition ( DiCarlo et al., 2012 ). The specific tasks within this 

group included naming the scene’s basic -level category (Tversky & Hemenway, 1983 ), 

naming its major objects ( Friedman, 1979 ), and providing a general scene description 

(Fei -Fei, 2007 ). As computer vision models are often optimized for these tasks (Krishna 

et al., 2017 ; Russakovsky et al., 2015 ), we expect that VLMs should approach human -

level performance in this group. 

Affordances, the set of possible actions that an agent could take in a scene, are foun -

dational to human visual understanding ( Gibson, 1986 ). These tasks were selected 

because scene affordances are understood by human observers very quickly and easily 

(Greene & Hansen, 2025 ; Greene & Oliva, 2009 ), despite requiring physical counterfac -

tual reasoning. The specific tasks in this group included listing a general list of possible 

actions one could take in the scene (Greene, Baldassano, Esteva, Beck, & Fei -Fei, 2016 ), 

providing a description of possible navigational paths ( Bonner & Epstein, 2017 ), and 

describing where a human could sit in each scene. For VLMs to succeed in these tasks, 

it will be necessary to reason about a body -scaled agent in a 3D scene from combining 

knowledge from 2D picture representations and distributional language training. 

The sensory experiences group probes knowledge that is grounded in cross -modal per -

ception rather than visual input alone. Human sensory perception is highly multimodal, 

and humans are able to reason about what they would hear, smell, and touch using 

only visual input ( Fassnidge & Freeman, 2018 ; Gottfried & Dolan, 2003 ; Keysers et 

al., 2004 ). The specific tasks in this group included generating a list of sounds, smells, 

and touch experiences they would experience in each scene ( Fassnidge & Freeman, 

2018 ; Gottfried & Dolan, 2003 ; Keysers et al., 2004 ), and providing an assessment of 

how loud each scene would be if one were there (@ Fassnidge & Freeman, 2018 ), and 

estimating of the physical temperature of each environment ( Greene & Oliva, 2009 ). 

VLMs may not perform well in these tasks, given that they lack access to cross -modal 

grounding. 

Affective responses to scenes are ecologically fundamental, shaping approach and avoid -

ance behavior in ways that are tightly coupled to scene identity (Russell & Pratt, 1980 ). 

Tasks in the affective experiences group included describing emotional experiences peo -

ple would have while in the environment (Olofsson, Nordin, Sequeira, & Polich, 2008 ), 

analyzing the extent to which they would feel safe in the environment ( Schroeder & 

Anderson, 1984 ), and assessing how aesthetically pleasing they found the environment 

(Vessel & Rubin, 2010 ). Because affective responses to environments are grounded 

in embodied experience of being in a space, they may represent dimensions of scene 

knowledge not captured by vision -language co -occurrence learning. 

Finally, scenes provide rich contextual information that supports prediction of future 

states ( Bar, 2004 ; Freyd, 1987 ). Consequently, the tasks in the future casting group 

required participants to mentally simulate different possible futures for each scene, 6

considering how quickly the scene’s appearance is changing ( Greene & Oliva, 2009 ), 

the amount of mystery versus predictability in the scene (Kaplan & Kaplan, 1989 ), and 

to describe how the scene will appear five minutes into the future ( Fouhey & Zitnick, 

2014 ). Each of these tasks requires temporal extrapolation beyond the current window, 

requiring causal knowledge of how environments and agents will interact. The precise 

instructions provided to human participants for each task are found in Table 1.

Human descriptions 

A total of 2,043 English -speaking participants were recruited to provide scene descrip -

tions from CloudResearch Connect (Hartman et al., 2023). 1,054 participants identified 

as women, 933 as men, 33 as non -binary, 6 as agender, 11 as members of non -listed 

gender minorities, and 6 as not disclosing their gender. Participants ranged in age from 

18 to 83 years (mean=37.7 years, median=36 years, sd = 12.5). 1902 participants were 

located in the United States, 69 in the United Kingdom, 63 in Canada, and three each 

in Australia, Ireland, and New Zealand. Each task was launched as a separate ex -

periment, and within each experiment, an individual could write as many descriptions 

as they wished that were still available from the pool. Each individual contributed 

between 1 and 41 descriptions (mean=2.9 descriptions, median=2 descriptions, sd =

3.03), and was compensated $0.25 per description. An additional 56 descriptions were 

collected but rejected and replaced because participants did not follow instructions or 

contained inappropriate language. Figure 1 shows representative descriptions for two 

example images across the 15 tasks.

Prompts 

To ensure that no model or task was disadvantaged by a single prompting strategy, we 

tested each model under three prompt conditions. The first prompt set was identical 

to the prompt provided to the human observers (see Table 1). The second set of 

prompts was engineered within GPT -4o -mini to produce the most qualitatively human -

like responses, as assessed qualitatively by the authors (GR and SS). The third prompt 

set was created by asking each model to produce a prompt that would elicit the most 

human -like responses on each task. Supplemental Item 2 contains the meta -prompts 

used to elicit prompts from all models. The final prompts provided by each model are in 

Supplemental Item 3. For each model and prompt, we elicited 100 unique descriptions 

for each of the 40 images, using the default temperature setting for each model. 

Embeddings 

Each description (human and VL model) was embedded using three embedding mod -

els: MPNet ( Song, Tan, Qin, Lu, & Liu, 2020 ), CLIP ( Radford et al., 2021 ), and 

RoBERTa ( Liu et al., 2019 ) to ensure that our results were not an artifact of any 

single embedding space, but instead replicated across complementary representational 

biases (sentence -level semantic similarity in MPNet/RoBERTa versus vision –language 

alignment in CLIP). Specifically, MPNet excels at sentence -level semantic similarity, 

while RoBERTa captures contextual language representations. Patterns that persist 

over all embedders likely reflect differences in VL model knowledge gained from their 

pretraining. 

Human -calibrated cosine distance (HCD) 

Many of the tasks we consider here require the observer to make predictions or write 

about subjective experiences for which there is no ground truth. Our goal, therefore, 

is to establish the extent to which VLMs reason about visual scenes in a human -like 

manner. To this end, we created a human -calibrated cosine distance (HCD) between 

human and VLM embedding vectors. This metric measures distance relative to within -

human variability, and ranges from statistical replaceability (i.e., as similar to human 11 

descriptions as humans are to each other) to being more dissimilar than a human 

description of an entirely different image. 

The HCD is computed as follows: 

HCD = dHM − LB 

UB − LB 

Where dHM is the median cosine distance between human and model embeddings, LB is 

the lower bound of human -human variability, and UB is the upper bound. The human -

model distances (dHM ) were computed as cosine distances between L2 -normalized em -

bedding vectors from human and VL model descriptions for each of the 15 prompt tasks 

(computed separately for each image and embedding type). We established upper and 

lower bounds relative to human -to -human description distances. First, we computed 

a per -image human “center” as the unit -normalized mean of the human embeddings. 

We then computed a lower bound to estimate within -image human –human variability: 

for each image, we iteratively left out one human observer and computed the cosine 

distance between the held -out human embedding and the centroid of the remaining 

human embeddings (leave -one -out, LOO). If VL –human distances approach this lower 

bound, VL model descriptions can be considered statistically indistinguishable from 

human responses. We also computed an upper bound intended to estimate across -

image dissimilarity: for each image, we computed cosine distances between its human 

embeddings and the centroids of all other images, and defined the upper bound as the 

95th percentile of these cross -image distances across images. If VL –human distances 

approach this upper bound, the VL descriptions are as dissimilar to the target image 

as a human description would be to an entirely different image. 

A human -calibrated cosine distance (HCD) of zero indicates full human replaceability 

(i.e., a model description that is statistically indistinguishable from human descrip -

tions), while a value of one indicates a description that is as different from the given 

image as an entirely different image. Furthermore, negative values can indicate that 

model descriptions cluster more tightly around the human centroid than individual 

human responses do, reflecting response homogeneity, and values greater than one 

indicate catastrophic failures that exceed the “different image” benchmark. 

Natural Language Processing (NLP) Metrics 

We assessed general aspects of human and VL model descriptions using more traditional 

NLP metrics as well. Using a pipeline that previously assessed the complexity of scene 

descriptions (Aronson, Adkins, & Greene, 2025 ; Greene & Trivedi, 2023 ), we extracted 

three features from each description: number of words, lexical entropy, and the average 

cosine similarity between word pairs in each description, as evaluated by Word2Vec 

(Mikolov, Sutskever, Chen, Corrado, & Dean, 2013 ). Finally, to quantify subjective 

information (e.g., emotions or attitudes), we performed a sentiment analysis to obtain 12 

an average sentiment score for each image within a task and within a model using the 

NLTK rule -based sentiment analysis tool, Vader ( Hutto & Gilbert, 2014 ). 

# Results 

Human Descriptions Are More Efficient 

We found that VL descriptions are longer but more lexically redundant than human 

descriptions, whereas humans achieve greater lexical diversity at matched length. On 

average, VL model descriptions were longer, with a human -generated response being 

14.4 words long while an average VL -generated response was 56.3 words long (see 

Figure 2A). We modeled log -transformed description length using a linear mixed -effects 

model with random intercepts for image and task. This model confirmed that VLM 

descriptions were significantly longer than human descriptions (β = 1.10 log -words, SE 

= 0.038, t(33550) = 29.21, p < 2e -16). 

Although VL models produced much more text overall, we found that the lexical 

variability of this text was lower than that of human descriptions. We fit a linear mixed 

effects model predicting lexical entropy from description type (humans versus VL). 

Because entropy grows with description length, we included centered log description 

length as a covariate, along with an interaction term for description type. As before, 

we included random intercepts for image and task. As expected, we found that entropy 

grows with description length ( β = 0.686, p < 2e -16), and that at the mean length, VL 

model descriptions had lower entropy than human descriptions (VL: β = -0.500, p < 2e -

16, see Figure 2B). Finally, VL model entropy increased more steeply with length than 

human entropy (Type x Length interaction: β = 0.190, p = 1e -8). In practice, over 

the range of observed description lengths, humans remain more lexically diverse than 

VL model outputs at matched length. This pattern is further supported by humans’ 

higher type -to -token ratio compared to VL models (M=0.89 versus 0.78, β = -0.11, p

< 2e -16, see Figure 2C). Finally, we examined the average cosine similarity between 

pairs of concepts within each description, as represented with Word2Vec (Mikolov et 

al., 2013 ). Overall, we found no main effect of description type (human versus VL, β =

0.0036, p = 0.204, see Figure 2D), but we found a significant effect of description length 

( β = -0.025, p < 2e -16) as well as an interaction between Type and Length ( β =

0.009, p=0.003), suggesting that Word2Vec cosine similarity decreased with description 

length, indicating increasing semantic diversity, but this decrease was significantly 

weaker for VL model text than for humans, suggesting that additional VL words add 

proportionally less new conceptual content. 

Sentiment analysis revealed that the overall tone of VLM descriptions was much more 

positive compared to human descriptions. The sentiment analysis score for each image, 

prompt, and task ranged from -100 (very negative) to +100 (very positive). We fit a 

linear mixed effects model predicting sentiment from description type (humans versus 13 

VL), and found that VL -generated texts scored much more positively than human -

generated content (human: 9.67, VL: 26.36, β = 16.69, p < 2e -16, see Figure 2E).

Human Descriptions are Sensitive to Task Hierarchies 

The representational geometry of human -generated description embeddings clustered 

hierarchically according to both specific task and task group (see Figure 3, left), rather 

than individual image identity (Figure 3, right). We quantified the apparent clustering 

structure using a k -nearest -neighbor (kNN) purity metric on the original embedding 

cosine distances at both the fine (15 tasks) and coarse (five superordinate groups) 

label levels. We first reduced the embedding dimensionality to the first 100 principal 

components for stability. Then, for each point i, we identified its k nearest neighbors 

(excluding itself) and calculated the fraction sharing i’s label, averaging across points 

for a final purity metric. We set k to equal 10% of the class size for each label (and 

have verified the robustness of this metric by sweeping k between 10% and 50%). We 14 

found that human scene descriptions had a kNN purity of 80% for the fine -grained 

tasks, and 91% for the superordinate task groups. 

Figure 3: t -distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t -SNE) projections of embed -

dings (MPNet) colored by task (left) and image (right). Embeddings cluster by task 

as opposed to image. 

We conducted the same analysis for our 18 models (selecting the best -performing model 

from each VL family for visualization in Figure 4). As shown in Figure 4, we observed 

a different pattern of task clustering for VL models compared to humans: whereas 

fine -grained purity levels were similar (80% for both), the model embeddings showed 

little additional structure at the coarse level: whereas human embeddings became 

markedly more homogeneous when labels were collapsed into the five superordinate 

groups (82% for models versus 91% for humans). To directly test whether humans 

and models diverged more at the coarse than fine level, we computed a delta score for 

each model, ∆ = (H − M )coarse − (H − M )fine , where positive values indicate greater 

human –model divergence at the coarse level. All 18 models yielded positive delta 

values (range: 0.020 –0.114), and the distribution of deltas was significantly greater 

than zero (Wilcoxon signed -rank test: V=171, p=7.63e -6). This pattern suggests that, 

in contrast to humans, VL models do not generate scene descriptions in a way that 

reflects the task hierarchy, yielding weaker within -task cohesion and greater mixing 

across higher -level groupings.

# Discussion 

We compared human and VL model generated text to a set of previously unreleased 

real -world photographs across a battery of high -level scene understanding tasks. We 

found that VLM descriptions were dramatically longer than human descriptions (~4x 

more words) but less informationally efficient: VLM descriptions exhibited lower lexical 

diversity across several measures (lower entropy, lower type -token ratio at matched 

length). Additionally, VLM descriptions had more positive sentiment compared to their 

human counterparts. A key finding was that affordance tasks yielded the least human -

like text from VLMs. Intriguingly, this deficit persisted across prompt engineering 

conditions, and were not improving in newer VLMs. 

Unsurprisingly, we found that, compared to providing VLMs with the same prompt 

as human observers, custom prompt engineering improved VLM outputs for affective, 

sensory, and future -casting tasks. This suggests that, for most tasks, VLMs have la -

tent capability that can be elicited by better prompts. Critically, we did not find 

that custom prompting improved the human likeness of affordance descriptions. Sim -

ilarly, although newer VLMs improved in their performance on sensory and affective 

tasks, we observed no significant improvement in affordance or future casting tasks 

over time. Whatever is driving general VLM improvement (more data, larger models, 

better training) is not addressing the affordance deficit. It therefore may be the case 

that scene affordance knowledge needs qualitatively different training from what has 

been standard. 

The low information density of VLM scene descriptions observed here extends pre -

vious observations of low information density in large language models (LLMs) more 

generally ( Guo et al., 2023 ; Shaib, Chakrabarty, Garcia -Olano, & Wallace, 2026 ). 

Although LLM -generated text can be rated more highly by human observers than 

human -generated text in domains such as creative writing ( Doshi & Hauser, 2024 ) or 

argumentative essays ( Herbold, Hautli -Janisz, Heuer, Kikteva, & Trautsch, 2023 ) that 

do not require specific factual details, LLM -generated text is generally characterized by 

internal redundancy and a paucity of specific details ( Shaib et al., 2026 ), colloquially 

referred to as “slop”. 23 

Altogether, the results of Experiment 1 demonstrate that although VLM -generated 

scene descriptions can be very humanlike in some tasks, a persistent gap exists for 

affordance tasks. The goal of Experiment 2 is to begin to understand why. We will 

begin by characterizing the structural and stylistic differences between human - and 

VLM -generated scene descriptions, and then test six different explanatory hypotheses 

for these differences. 

# Experiment 2: Explaining Performance Patterns 

# Across Tasks 

Experiment 1 demonstrated that, across models, prompt strategies, and encoders, VL 

models struggled to describe scene affordances in a human -like way. The goal of Experi -

ment 2 was to provide insights into why this might be. Figure 7 illustrates our approach. 

In Section 1, we will characterize the differences between VL - and human -generated 

text using NLP to assess the differences between human and VLM affordance -based 

scene descriptions. In Section 2, we will explore six different mechanistic hypotheses to 

explain why VL models generate descriptions of scene affordances that markedly differ 

from human observers’. 

# Characterizing the differences in affordance descriptions 

Do VL models differ stylistically from humans in affordance tasks? 

We examined whether affordance task descriptions were outliers for VL models in any 

of our NLP metrics. Any differences here would reflect stylistic differences between VL 

models and humans that uniquely affected the affordance tasks. 

We observed no significant differences in log description length between any of the 

task groups (all corrected pairwise p>0.25, see Supplemental Item 4). Further, we 

observed no differences in lexical entropy across task groups (all corrected pairwise 

p>0.20, see Supplemental Item 5). Similarly, the affordance tasks did not differ signifi -

cantly from other tasks according to Word2vec cosine similarity (all corrected pairwise 

p>0.17, see Supplemental Item 6). Finally, we observed no significant sentiment differ -

ences between affordance descriptions and the descriptions for other tasks (all corrected 

pairwise p>0.77, see Supplemental Item 7). Therefore, human -VL affordance descrip -

tion differences were not driven by differences in style that included description length, 

description complexity, or sentiment, and therefore reflect deeper, more structural dif -

ferences between VLMs and humans. 24

Are VL affordance differences characterized by catastrophic failures? 

The NLP analyses ruled out purely stylistic reasons for the affordance performance 

gap. We next sought to assess the extent to which poor performance reflected a genuine 

failure to understand the affordance tasks. If VL models’ failures with the affordance 

tasks represent a true lack of capability in these tasks, then we should observe a higher 

rate of catastrophic failures to replicate human descriptions (i.e., HCD values greater 

than 1), resulting from hallucinations, task refusal, or other serious deficits. 

Human -calibrated distances could be greater than one, reflecting cases where the aver -

age VL model response for a given image was more different from the human response 

than human responses for an entirely different image was to the human response. If 

VL models’ poorer performance on affordance tasks is due to a foundational lack of 

task understanding, then we would expect a higher proportion of HCD values above 

one for affordances than for other tasks. 

To test this, we examined the proportion of HCD values exceeding 1 across Task 

Groups, Prompts, Model Families, and Time since Model Release. This model had a

conditional R2 of 0.62, with a marginal R2 of 0.53 attributable to fixed effects alone. 

The model’s intercept, corresponding to Task Group = general knowledge, Prompt =

human, Time since Model Release = 0 and Model Family = Claude, is at -4.42 (95% 

CI [-5.98, -2.87], p < .001). This corresponds to a baseline catastrophic failure rate of 

1.2%. 

We found that catastrophic failures were largely driven by the affordance and sensory 

tasks. While all Task Groups showed low proportions of greater -than -one HCDs, gen -

eral knowledge, affective, and future casting had the lowest proportions (1.2%, 4.9%, 

and 0.2%, respectively). Affordance and sensory tasks accounted for 6.2% and 8.6%, 

respectively. Within Task Groups, relative to the general knowledge baseline task, 

we found that affordances and sensory tasks showed significantly higher catastrophic 

failure rates (affordances: β = 2.09, 95% CI [0.28, 3.90], p = 0.024, sensory: β = 2.47, 

95% CI[0.67, 4.26], p = 0.007). By contrast, affective and future casting did not differ 

significantly from the general knowledge baseline (affective: β = 1.34, 95% CI [ -0.47, 

3.15], p = 0.148, future casting: β = -1.64, 95% CI [-4.28, 1.00], p = 0.222). Follow -up 

pairwise tests using Holm correction revealed that affordances had higher proportions 

than future casting (affordances - future casting = 3.73, p = 0.031) and sensory had 

higher proportions than future casting (future casting - sensory= -4.11, p = 0.012). 

This means that affordances and sensory tasks were more likely to output responses 

that were less similar to human responses than an entirely different image, likely re -

flecting a fundamental issue in the VL models’ understanding of the tasks. Overall, 

although affordance tasks had a higher rate of catastrophic failures than some tasks, 

sensory tasks were also difficult by this metric. Therefore, the deficits in VL models’ 

affordance descriptions are likely to have multiple causes. 

Catastrophic failures also varied between prompt conditions and model families. 

Within prompt conditions, we found that, compared to the human prompt, there 26 

were significantly higher greater -than -one HCD proportions for the custom prompt 

(β = -1.87, 95% CI [-2.63, -1.10], p = 1.61e -06). The VL -generated prompt was not 

significantly different from the human prompt. Within model families, both Deepseek 

and Gemini differed significantly from the Claude baseline. Deepseek had significantly 

higher greater -than -one HCD proportions (β = 1.33, 95% CI [0.63, 2.04], p = 0.0002) 

and Gemini had significantly lower greater -than -one HCD proportions ( β = -2.89, 

95% CI [ -4.94, -0.84], p = 0.006). There was no main effect of Time Since Model 

Release. Therefore, while prompting can prevent catastrophic failures to a certain 

extent, model differences are widespread and there is no strong evidence that models 

are improving in this metric over time. 

# Mechanisms of VL and human differences in affordances 

The previous analyses indicated that the affordance -based scene description differences 

observed in Experiment 1 were driven more by structural failures than superficial stylis -

tic differences. The goal of this section is to explore several mechanistic hypotheses that 

could explain these differences. Here, we roughly order the hypotheses by the depth of 

representational failure, with early hypotheses reflecting engineering constraints that 

are likely to be resolved with increasing resources, and later hypotheses reflecting a 

foundational lack of affordance -based scene understanding in VL models. 

Hypothesis 1: Performance across tasks could be attributed to prompt 

length 

Some models (especially in the Deepseek family) had very small context windows (see 

Supplemental Item 1). If a prompt’s length approaches the VLM’s context window 

limit, then fewer tokens can be used in the scene description, and this could lead to a

less human -like performance. If this is the case, we would expect a negative correlation 

between prompt length and a VL model’s HCD. More specifically, we hypothesized 

that if prompt length is driving the previously -observed task differences, we would 

observe longer prompts for the affordance tasks. In order to test this hypothesis, 

we compared the length of each prompt (in characters) across task groups. Prompt 

length varied from an average of 924 characters for affective to 1124 for future casting. 

However, compared with a baseline for general knowledge tasks, we observed no reliable 

differences across task groups (all p > 0.4; see Supplemental Item 8 for all statistics). 

Therefore, the task -related differences we observed cannot be attributed to prompt 

length. 27 

Hypothesis 2: VL models express more uncertainty for affordance tasks 

Alternatively, VL models may exhibit greater expressions of uncertainty in affordance 

tasks than in other task groups. To test this hypothesis, we created a lexicon of 

32 words associated with hedging behavior (e.g., “maybe”, “possibly”, “seems”, see 

Supplemental Item 9 for complete list), and compared the proportion of descriptions 

with hedging language across tasks. To model hedging as a continuous outcome while 

respecting its bounded range [0,1], we applied a logit transformation to the hedging 

proportion. Because the logit is undefined at 0 and 1, we first winsorized the propor -

tions to lie within [ ϵ, 1 -ϵ] using a small constant ϵ = 10e -6. Using a linear mixed -effects 

model with random intercepts for Task and Image, we found that human descriptions 

contained significantly less hedging than VL model outputs (β = -0.464 on the logit 

scale, SE = 0.136, t(32945) = -3.43, p = 0.00061), corresponding to ~37% lower hedg -

ing odds. This suggests that VL models’ descriptions can be characterized by a good 

deal of expressive uncertainty overall. To test whether affordance tasks dispropor -

tionally caused this hedging behavior, we fit a linear mixed -effects model predicting 

logit -transformed hedging rate from Task Group, with random intercepts for Task and 

Model. Estimated marginal means and Tukey -adjusted pairwise comparisons showed 

that general knowledge descriptions contained significantly less hedging than affective 

(∆logit = 4.50, p = .0126), future -casting (∆logit = 5.30, p = .0016), and sensory 

tasks (∆logit = 4.54, p = .0112), consistent with these task groups eliciting more un -

certainty/qualification language overall. In contrast, affordances did not differ reliably 

from general knowledge (∆logit = 2.08, p = .578) and did not differ from the other 

task groups after correction (all p > .15). Thus, while hedging varies substantially 

by task demands, affordance tasks are not uniquely characterized by elevated hedging, 

suggesting that VL models’ differences in affordance tasks was not driven by a higher 

expression of uncertainty. 

Hypothesis 3: VL models’ affordance knowledge is shallow and associative 

If VL models’ poorer performance on affordance tasks is due to shallow, associative 

affordance knowledge, then one might expect that VL descriptions contain more stereo -

typed and generic responses for affordance tasks compared to others. In this case, we 

would expect a higher proportion of negative HCD values than in other tasks. Negative 

HCD values occur in cases when human responses were more diverse than those pro -

duced by the VL models. We examined the proportion of negative HCD values across 

Task Groups, Prompts, Model Families, and Time since Model Release, including Task 

as a random effect. This model had a conditional R2 of 0.39, with a marginal R2 of 

0.35 attributable to fixed effects alone. The model’s intercept, corresponding to Task 

Group = general knowledge, Prompt = human, Time since Model Release = 0, and 

Model Family = Claude, is at -2.58 (95% CI [-3.20, -1.97], p < .001). This corresponds 

to an overall negative HCD proportion of 7.5%. 28 

Interestingly, we found general knowledge and affordances had a relatively low percent -

age of negative HCDs (14.0% and 13.8%, respectively). By contrast, affective (40.7%), 

future casting (54.7%, and sensory (37.4%) elicited more negative HCDs. Within Task 

Groups, relative to the general knowledge baseline task, we found that affordances did 

not differ significantly in the proportion of negative HCDs ( β = -0.001, 95% CI [-0.63, 

0.63], p = 0.996). By contrast, affective ( β = 1.67, 95% CI [1.07, 2.27], p = 5.68e -08), 

future casting ( β = 2.36, 95% CI [1.75, 2.96], p = 2.06e -14), and sensory ( β = 1.50, 

95% CI [0.89, 2.10], p = 1.15e -06 tasks had higher proportions of negative HCDs rel -

ative to general knowledge. Follow -up pairwise tests using Holm correction revealed 

that these affordances also have significantly lower proportions than affective (affective 

- affordances = 1.67, p < 0.0001), future casting (affordances - future casting = -2.36, 

p < 0.0001), and sensory (affordances - sensory = -1.50, p = <0.0001). This indicates 

that affordance descriptions from VL models were more lexically diverse than those for 

affective, future -casting, and sensory tasks, but not different from general knowledge 

tasks. 

We also found diversity in the negative HCD rate across prompt conditions and model 

families. Within the prompt conditions, we found that, compared to the human

the two -dimensional picture, and thus disproportionately struggle to describe scene 

affordances. To test this hypothesis, we replaced the images in the prompts with 

spatially explicit scene descriptions. 

One author (MRG) created general descriptions for each scene that explicitly provided 

absolute and relative spatial information. For example, for the image on the right -hand 

side of Figure 6, “You are near the front of an elaborate church or cathedral. There are 

chairs immediately in front of you and to the left. The altar is in front of you, about 

25 meters away (and up two shallow stairs). There is seating on either side of the altar 

about 5 -10 meters away. Beyond the altar is a pipe organ. The walls on the far side 

have elaborate carvings and there are stained glass windows near the ceiling. The floor 

is marble tile.” These descriptions were meant to be more detailed than the general 

scene descriptions obtained online (typical example of the same image: “inside a large 

church, some wooden chairs at the front with a pulpit further back”). We provided 

these descriptions to six VL models (the most recently released model within each 

family), along with human -prompt instructions for the navigation and sitting tasks. 

Human -calibrated cosine distances were computed as in Experiment 1. 

If HCD values improve when replacing the image with a spatially -explicit text de -

scription, this would indicate that the visual module contains insufficient spatial infor -

mation, but that affordance information could be recovered when this information is 

provided via text. However, we did not find evidence of a benefit: whereas the original 

average HCD was 0.16 across the two tasks, the HCDs for the spatially -explicit text 

increased to 0.22, indicating that the photograph provided helpful information to the 

VLMs. A mixed -effects model predicting HCD from Condition (text versus image), 

Task (navigation versus sitting), and their interaction, with random intercepts for Im -

age and Model, revealed no main effect of Condition (β = 0.001, 95% CI [-0.007, 0.009], 

p = 0.77) but a strong Condition x Task interaction ( β = -0.13, 95% CI [ -0.14, -0.12], 

p < 2e -16). For navigation descriptions, the intervention had essentially no effect on 

HCD. For sitting descriptions, however, the intervention substantially increased HCD 

relative to the original prompt, indicating less human -like responses. 

To ensure that the spatially explicit scene descriptions contained sufficient information 

to replace the original images for the affordance tasks, we conducted a validation study 

with human participants. 800 participants were recruited on Cloud ResearchConnect 

to provide descriptions for the sitting and navigation tasks based on the spatially ex -

plicit scene descriptions instead of the images. 445 participants identified as women, 

329 as men, 18 as non -binary, 1 as agender, 4 as members of non -listed gender mi -

norities, and 3 as not disclosing their gender. Participants ranged in age from 18 to 

78 years (mean=37.6 years, median = 35 years, sd = 12.33). Other than replacing 31 

the images with scene descriptions, the participants’ instructions remained unchanged. 

Each individual contributed between 1 and 40 descriptions to provide a total of 10 hu -

man descriptions per image per task. If human observers could provide equally good 

responses with either the image or the longer text, we would expect HCD values of 

0.0. We found that the average HCD between the text descriptions and the original 

image descriptions for humans was 0.11 (sitting HCD = 0.04, navigation HCD = 0.18). 

Therefore, although human observers benefitted from the image, the fact that HCDs 

in the text condition were lower than the VL models in the text condition suggests 

that the spatially explicit scene descriptions contain sufficient information for humans 

to recover affordance information. Given that the spatially explicit descriptions were 

sufficient to recover affordance information but the VL models were unable to make 

use of it, we conclude that their affordance description differences were not due to a

failure of the models’ visual encoders. 

Hypothesis 5: VL models fail to describe affordances because they do not 

automatically project themselves into the three -dimensional structure of a 

scene from a photograph 

Affordance understanding is an intrinsically egocentric act. Therefore, if a VL model 

does not automatically project itself into the three -dimensional structure of a scene, it 

may not be able to express affordance -related information. To test this, we provided 

the same six models with amended human prompts for affordance tasks that explicitly 

provided viewpoint information. 

We amended the prompts to include information on the viewer’s position within the 

scene. For example, the amended navigation prompt read “ You are standing where 

the camera is, facing into the scene. Imagine that you are telling a blind person next 

to you how to walk through this scene. Write a short (1 -3 sentence) description that 

could instruct such a person.” (Italicized text shows addition from original prompt). 

We provided this prompt to the same six models from Hypothesis 4, and obtained 

descriptions, computing human -calibrated cosine distances as in Experiment 1. 

Interestingly, we found that providing viewpoint information provided an overall benefit 

to VL models. A mixed -effects model predicting human -calibrated cosine distance 

from Condition (original versus amended), Task (navigation versus sitting), and their 

interaction, with random intercepts for image and model, revealed a significant main 

effect of Condition (β = 0.007, 95% CI [3.09e -04, 0.01], p = 0.04), indicating significant 

improvement in HCD with the amended prompt. Further, we found that the sitting 

task led to consistently worse performance compared to navigation (β = 0.08, 95% 

CI [0.07, 0.08], p < 2e -16), and a significant interaction between condition and task, 

indicating that the amended prompt improved performance in the sitting task more 

than navigation ( β = -0.14, 95% CI [ -0.15, -0.13], p < 2e -16). In a separate analysis, 

we examined the rates of generic responses (negative HCDs) and catastrophic failures 32 

(HCDs>1) as a function of prompt condition. We found that the original prompt 

condition increased the rate of catastrophic failures (β = 0.23, 95% CI [0.04, 0.42], p

= 0.017), but had no effect on the rate of negative HCD responses (β = 0.01, 95% 

CI [ -0.05, 0.07], p = 0.74), compared to the amended prompt. Therefore, explicitly 

specifying viewpoint information can aid human -VLM alignment on affordance tasks, 

but its benefit is highly task -specific. 

Hypothesis 6: VL models’ training data contains sparse information about 

scene affordances 

Humans are highly sensitive to scene affordances ( Gibson, 1986 ; Greene et al., 2016 ;

Greene & Hansen, 2025 ), and Gricean maxims dictate that humans do not explain 

things that are self -evident to other humans ( Grice, 1991 ). Therefore, image caption 

datasets used to train VL models may have comparatively less information about scene 

affordances. The lack of scaffolding of affordance information in language could cause 

lower performance in the affordance tasks. 

Although training data for many closed weight VLMs has not been specified, image 

captions provide one type of information that is likely part of all VLM training regimes. 

We examined two image captioning datasets to test this hypothesis. The Conceptual 

12M dataset (Changpinyo, Sharma, Ding, & Soricut, 2021 ) is a set of 12 million image -

text pairs sourced from various internet sources, and Redcaps (Desai, Kaul, Aysola, 

& Johnson, 2021 ) is a set of an additional 12 million image -text pairs sourced from 

Reddit. Our approach was to create three lexicons containing words that could be used 

in affordance descriptions: permission/constraint (e.g., “allow”, “can”, “caution”, etc.), 

instrumentality (e.g., “use”, “tool”, “enable”, etc.), and accessibility (e.g., “narrow”, 

“path”, “open”, etc.), for a total of 150 affordance -related terms. As a control, we 

created two lexicons of affective concepts (20 positive, 20 negative). We reasoned 

that, because affective tasks were comparatively easier for VL models, a lower rate 

of affordance terms compared to affective terms might have resulted from statistics of 

their training sets. To find a set of 40 affordance terms with similar word frequency to 

the affective term list, we used a quartile matching procedure. We first estimated the 

word frequency of each affect term using the Brown corpus ( Kučera & Francis, 1967 )

as a reference corpus. We then computed empirical quantiles from this frequency 

distribution. For each target quantile, q, we computed the corresponding frequency 

value, f, from the affective lexicon and selected words from the affordance candidate 

pool whose frequency was closest to f, enforcing selection without replacement to ensure 

that each candidate word could be chosen at most once. This yielded a set of 40 

affordance terms whose frequency distribution closely tracked that of the 40 affect 

terms. The full set of words in each lexicon can be found in Supplemental Item 10. 

In both image caption datasets, matched terms were slightly more likely to belong to 

the affective lexicon than the affordance (CC12M: 52% affective; RedCaps: 56% affec -

tive), despite being matched for baseline word frequency. We compared this rate to the 33 

affordance -heavy descriptions from WikiHow ( Koupaee & Wang, 2018 ), finding that 

only 36% of matched terms were affective, indicating that our affordance lexicon does 

indeed predominate when affordances are being conveyed. While this analysis demon -

strates that there might be slightly less affordance information in image captioning 

sets, this modest imbalance alone is unlikely to explain VLMs’ pronounced affordance 

deficits. 

Affordances are complex relationships between objects and possible actions, and it is 

difficult to identify them using simple distributional semantics. To test whether im -

age caption datasets contain affordance vocabulary in relational (agent -action -object) 

constructions, we analyzed the syntactic structure of affordance term usage across 

CC12M and RedCaps datasets. We used the same affordance and affective lexicons 

described above and extracted all sentences containing at least one term from either 

lexicon. For each term occurrence, we used the dependency parser from the spaCy 

library (en_core_web_sm) to extract syntactic features indicative of relational affor -

dance language: (1) whether the term functioned as a verb versus a noun or adjective, 

(2) whether the sentence contained second -person pronouns (“you,” “your”), indicat -

ing an addressed experiencer, (3) whether the term appeared in a modal construction 

(“can sit,” “should use”), encoding possibility or permission, (4) whether the term 

governed a spatial prepositional complement (“walk through the door”), (5) whether 

the sentence was imperative (verb -initial with no explicit subject), and (6) whether the 

term appeared in a purpose clause (“to open,” “for walking”). 

As shown in Table 5, the WikiHow dataset generally contained significantly more of 

the six affordance construction features than did either of the image caption datasets.

evidence that the affordance deficit was shallow or stylistic. Instead, we found evidence 

that the deficits were more structural and semantic. 

We found that our characterizations and explanations that had less conceptual depth 

did not have evidence: VL model descriptions for the affordance task group did not 

differ in linguistic style from other task groups, the prompt lengths for affordance tasks 

were not significantly different than other tasks, the VL models did not express more 

uncertainty for affordance tasks, the descriptions were not more stereotyped (i.e, no 

more negative HCDs), and the failures did not seem to be due to failures in the visual 

encoding model since removing the need for the visual encoder by swapping out images 

for spatial descriptions did not improve results. 

By contrast, our results pointed to alternatives that suggested that affordance deficits 

were deeper and more structural. We found that affordance tasks differed reliably in 

terms of the rate of catastrophic failures from most other tasks: VLMs for affordances 

had higher rates of HCDs that were greater than 1, as did sensory tasks. This indicates 

that VLMs will frequently provide text that is more different from human descriptions 

than a description of an entirely different image. In other words, these failures reflect 

fundamental model misunderstandings in these tasks. 

We tested two interventions to see if they improved the VLMs’ performance for the 

affordance tasks. We found that adding language to the prompt about the viewer’s 

position within the scene helped some models in some tasks produce output that is 

more similar to human output. We also found that replacing the images with spa -

tial descriptions did not improve VLM output similarity to humans. These results 

show that while spatial information alone is not enough for VLMs to mirror human 

responses, including information that primes the VLM that there is an agent and the 

agent has a size and a perspective can yield better results. This ties to our finding 

that while affordance language is present in image captioning corpora, the majority 

of this language does not include affordance information in relation to an agent. One 

remaining possibility is that VLMs fail at affordances because they do not have a nat -

ural understanding of the physicality of an agent like humans do which is necessary for 

completing these affordance tasks that are about what an agent can do in a space. 

None of these characterizations or hypotheses on their own fully characterize and ex -

plain the difference between affordance tasks and other tasks that leads to less human -

like affordance descriptions. However, taken together, our findings paint a picture that 

shows that VLMs lack a deep understanding of what it’s like to be an embodied agent, 

which may be fundamental to performing certain tasks, such as affordance tasks. 36 

# General Discussion 

Human scene understanding is deeply linked not only to purely visual processing, 

but also to a rich, embodied experience of other sensory content, affordances and 

motor planning, predictions of upcoming events, and our affective reactions to the 

environment. What information is sufficient to learn this rich set of associations? In 

this work, we used vision -language models (VLMs) to examine the extent to which 

scene understanding can be achieved through the joint learning of two -dimensional 

pictures and text descriptions. In two experiments, we found that performance differed 

markedly across task types, implying that scene understanding is not unitary. Some 

information, especially general knowledge, emotional reasoning, and future prediction, 

appear to be learnable from image -language statistics. However, VLMs persistently 

struggle to describe scene affordances in a human -like manner. Further, affordance 

tasks remain harder across prompting conditions, and are not well -explained by shallow, 

stylistic differences, including the expression of uncertainty or the genericness of the 

responses. Further, VLM performance on affordance tasks is not improving in newer 

models. This work evokes previous results with large language models (LLMs), who 

show deficits in affordance reasoning in text inputs ( Jones et al., 2022 ). 

While this work demonstrates that the performance gap for affordance tasks is unlikely 

to have a single cause, the explorations of the phenomenon in Experiment 2 collectively 

point to VLMs’ reduced ability to project themselves as a human -like agent in a three -

dimensional scene from the two -dimensional picture itself. We do not believe that this 

is a simple failure of the models’ visual encoders: when images were replaced with 

spatially -explicit text that was sufficient for human observers to generate affordances, 

VLMs were not able to generate better affordance descriptions. Rather, we found that 

affordance performance improved in many cases when we provided information that 

explicitly located the viewer at a camera position within a three -dimensional scene. 

Taken together, these two results imply that merely handing models more spatial text 

is not enough, but explicitly specifying the viewer’s position can help. However, this 

level of improvement was task - and model -specific, and thus did not fully explain the 

performance gap. Finally, we found that affordance -relevant language is comparatively 

sparse in image caption data used for training data, particularly in agent -addressed, 

action -guiding constructions. 

This pattern of results can be explained with two complementary theoretical frame -

works. The first, the Gricean hypothesis, posits that foundation models, including 

VLMs and LLMs should exhibit difficulties precisely in the tasks that are easiest for 

human observers. Specifically, humans do not describe things that are self -evident to 

other humans ( Grice, 1991 ). Because human observers are highly sensitive to scene 

affordances ( Gibson, 1986 ; Greene et al., 2016 ; Greene & Hansen, 2025 ), it might be 

the case that human -generated language contains sparse and compressed affordance 

information that does not support model learning about affordances. We found that 

affordance -based information was comparatively sparse in image -captioning datasets, 37 

particularly constructions where agents are addressed in the second person. However, 

given the correlational nature of these results, and their comparatively modest effect 

sizes, it’s unlikely that Gricean language compression is the sole cause of the affordance 

performance gap. 

The second, the embodiment hypothesis, holds that affordances cannot be learned 

from distributional semantics or flat photographs — they must be grounded in three -

dimensional experience. This follows Gibson’s original claim that affordances are di -

rectly perceived through invariant features arising from bodily movement through the 

environment ( Gibson, 1986 ). This implies that no two -dimensional proxy can fully 

substitute for embodied, three -dimensional experience. While the strongest versions 

of these claims remain controversial, empirical evidence in both human and machine 

vision supports them. First, compared to two -dimensional images, real objects elicit 

very different neural responses ( Snow & Culham, 2021 ), and attentional engagement 

(Gomez, Skiba, & Snow, 2018 ). Second, object learning in typically -developing infants 

(Soska et al., 2010 ) as well as children whose vision has been surgically restored in late 

childhood (Ostrovsky et al., 2009 ) is linked to viewing objects in different positions via 

self motion. Finally, some in computer vision have argued that meaning is a result of 

grounding symbolic representations via multimodal perceptual experiences of concepts 

in the world ( Bisk et al., 2020 ), and agentive exploratory experience seems beneficial 

to VLM learning ( Zhang et al., 2026 ). 

In these experiments, VLMs showed underperformance with sensory as well as affor -

dance tasks. Previous work has shown that LLMs use sensory language in a manner 

that differs from human observers (Hicke, Hamilton, & Mimno, 2025 ), although models 

vary in how they differ from humans. However, LLM and VLM performance across 

sensory tasks is not uniform. While models show strong human alignment in basic 

sensory features, including color, pitch, and loudness (Kawakita, Zeleznikow -Johnston, 

Tsuchiya, & Oizumi, 2024 ; Marjieh et al., 2024 ), other sensory features, including taste, 

musical timbre, smell, and tactile features show low human alignment ( Marjieh et al., 

2024 ; Zhong, Zhou, Dawes, Brianz, & Obrist, 2024 ). Interestingly, VLM alignment 

is not stronger than LLM alignment, even in visual tasks ( Marjieh et al., 2024 ), sug -

gesting that most sensory knowledge in foundation models is gained from text. Future 

work can examine the extent to which certain types of sensory information are present 

in training corpora to disentangle the extent to which non -alignment is due to sparse 

training information, as would be predicted by the Gricean hypothesis, versus the need 

to train with information beyond text, such as embodied world experience. 

Our results also provide insights into applied aspects of VLM use in psychology. For 

example, our results demonstrate that VLMs diverge markedly from human observers 

in terms of writing style. Specifically, VLMs generate far more text, but with less 

information density compared to human observers, consistent with the characteriza -

tion of generative AI -derived content as “slop” ( Shaib et al., 2026 ). Critically, these 

stylistic differences are not task -dependent: explorations in Experiment 2 found that 

stylistic differences could not explain the performance patterns across tasks. These 38 

results also highlight the need to show human -relative semantic alignment, such as we 

did through the human -calibrated cosine distance metric, to disentangle stylistic dif -

ferences from semantic ones. We also observed some illuminating interactions between 

semantics and style across tasks. For example, VLMs exhibited lower rates of hedging 

language for both general knowledge and affordance tasks, likely reflecting the fact 

that these tasks require more objective information than more speculative tasks, such 

as those in the future casting or affective groups. However, the VLMs’ performance 

in general knowledge tasks exceeds their performance in the affordance tasks, making 

these descriptions uniquely read as “confidently wrong”. This pattern of results echoes 

the charge that generative AI produces “bullshit” in the sense of philosopher Harry 

Frankfurt — highly fluent and confident prose that is utterly unconcerned with its own 

veracity ( Gorrieri, 2024 ; Hicks, Humphries, & Slater, 2024 ). It may also be the case 

that the relative success of VLMs in more subjective tasks can be attributed to this 

proclivity, or reflective of the fact that human observers and VLMs speculate in similar 

ways. In this way, our results concur with other recent work that demonstrates that 

VLMs can emulate human -like visual affective reasoning (Conwell, Graham, Boccagno, 

& Vessel, 2025 ). 

These results also have implications for psychologists and cognitive neuroscientists who 

have begun to use VLMs as a research tool. VL models have been used to annotate 

ground truth categories ( Rong et al., 2025 ), emotional features such as facial expres -

sions ( Niu et al., 2025 ), and semantic descriptions ( Murlidaran & Eckstein, 2025 ). 

Quality model -generated content could help scale cognitive research, but it is neces -

sary to assess the limitations of these models as well. Our data suggest that some 

tasks, such as general knowledge tasks, can be done very well by VLMs, although 

they fall short of fully human -interchangeable by our HCD metrics. However, for re -

searchers interested in affordance and sensory tasks, model -generated content should 

not substitute for human descriptions. 

Although this work has analyzed human and VLM -generated descriptions on the order 

of hundreds of millions of words, there are critical limitations that should be considered. 

Firstly, our experiment set was limited to 40 real -world images. Although they were 

selected to represent a diverse range of places, the small set may not generalize to 

larger stimulus sets. Further, resource and time constraints required that some of 

the follow -up interventions from Experiment 2 were done only on a subset of models, 

limiting inferences that could be made about specific architectures or training regimes 

playing a causal role in performance patterns. Our design aimed to examine patterns 

of performance across a broad range of high -level scene tasks. Given the marked 

deficits for affordances, as well as the differences observed between affordance tasks in 

Experiment 2, it would be illuminating to examine a more diverse range of affordance 

tasks in future work. Finally, this work examined human -VLM alignment through 

assessing the semantic similarity in their generated descriptions. Future work can also 

examine the internal model states to assess the extent to which affordance information 

is represented, but not expressed. 39 

Across 18 VLM models from six different model families, and across a broad range of 

15 high -level scene understanding tasks, we observed that although VLMs can generate 

human -like scene descriptions in many cases, persistent gaps exist for affordance and 

sensory tasks that are structural and semantic rather than stylistic. The affordance 

gap is particularly telling: it resists prompting interventions, does not improve with 

model scale, and cannot be attributed to training data sparsity alone. Taken together, 

these findings suggest that the path to human -like scene understanding may require 

more than scaling what already exists. To understand an environment’s action possi -

bilities may require the kind of agent -centered, three -dimensional experience that no 

photograph and no caption can fully encode. Whether that gap is bridgeable through 

simulation, embodied robotics, or some representation we have not yet imagined re -

mains an open question. But the present results suggest it is a real gap, and one worth 

taking seriously.
